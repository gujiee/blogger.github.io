Cordyceps, also known as winter worm summer grass, is explained in the encyclopedia as follows: Cordyceps sinensis, also called Chinese cordyceps, is a traditional precious Chinese medicinal material. It is formed when a parasitic fungus of the genus Cordyceps infects the larvae of ghost moths in alpine meadow soil. The larva becomes mummified, and under suitable conditions in summer, a long rod-shaped fruiting body sprouts from the head of the dead larva — forming a复合体 of the fungus's fruiting body and the mummified larva. It is mainly produced in the high, cold regions and snowy grasslands of China's Qinghai, Tibet, Xinjiang, Sichuan, Yunnan, Gansu, and Guizhou provinces.

This was the most scientific information I had about cordyceps. I also knew that many officials and wealthy people loved this stuff — steeping it in tea or liquor, hoping to prolong their lives. Consequently, the price of cordyceps, like Hermès and Louis Vuitton, was always sky-high, far beyond the reach of ordinary people like me. Near the Xining train station, there was a dedicated cordyceps market. I had been there. A catty of top-grade cordyceps cost 70,000 yuan. A catty of ordinary cordyceps was around 30,000 yuan.

From a scientific perspective, I found it hard to believe that a dead worm could contain some magical substance capable of prolonging life. But I was not a scientist, nor a doctor. What did my opinion matter?

During my trip to the Northwest, a local friend took me into the mountains to see the cordyceps harvest. We drove for hours, the road growing increasingly narrow and rough. Finally, we abandoned the car and continued on foot. The air was thin, the sun fierce, and every step on the sloping grassland was exhausting. After two hours of climbing, we reached an altitude of over 4,000 meters.

The cordyceps collectors were already there — Tibetan men and women, their faces weathered by wind and sun, their hands calloused. They crawled on all fours across the grass, their eyes scanning the ground inch by inch. A cordyceps, before it sprouts, is nothing but a tiny bump on the surface — easy to miss, especially among the dense grass of the plateau. The collectors searched with the patience of miners, for this was indeed gold — gold worth 70,000 yuan a catty.

I watched a middle-aged woman find one. She carefully dug around it with a small pick, loosening the soil, then gently pulled it out with her fingers. She held it up to the sun, examining it with narrowed eyes, then placed it carefully into a pouch at her waist. Her face showed no excitement — just the calm satisfaction of a job well done.

"How many can you find in a day?" I asked.

"On a good day, a dozen or so. On a bad day... nothing."

She had been doing this for ten years. Ten years of crawling on all fours across the plateau, searching for dead worms that might or might not prolong someone's life.

As I walked back down the mountain, I thought of the officials and wealthy people who paid 70,000 yuan for a catty of cordyceps. I thought of the woman crawling on the grass, searching for hours. I thought of the long chain connecting them — the fungus, the larva, the soil, the sun, the collector, the merchant, the wealthy customer. All of them, in one way or another, were searching for something. Immortality, perhaps. Or simply a better life.

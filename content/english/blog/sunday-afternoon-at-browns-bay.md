Today is February 22, 2026. In the afternoon, I had just parked my car in the New World supermarket lot. The moment I pushed open the door, I was greeted not only by the slightly salty sea breeze, but also by the mingled sounds of waves crashing, children laughing, and cheerful, rhythmic music.

Walking along Anzac Road to the roundabout, then turning into Clyde Road, within a few steps I reached a small square. It was tiny — less than ten meters wide, twenty or thirty meters long. More of a very short pedestrian street than a real square. At its southwest corner sat a small Starbucks, which I privately called "Starbucks Square."

At the entrance to the square, a small outdoor stage had been set up with two street performers. The man, guitar slung over his shoulder, bent his head to accompany; the woman sang with abandon, her voice bright and joyful, drawing many passersby to stop. Some brought hot drinks from the coffee shop and sat on the nearby benches, listening contentedly. Others stepped forward and dropped coins into a cardboard box, a gesture of appreciation for the beautiful music.

Next to Starbucks was the famous "Penguino" ice cream shop. Their vanilla ice cream consistently won gold and silver at Auckland's ice cream competitions. This season, the shop was always packed, with lines snaking out the door.

I bought a cone and walked toward the beach. Browns Bay was bathed in golden sunlight. The sea sparkled, children ran along the shoreline, dogs chased balls, and a few sailboats drifted on the distant horizon. I found a spot on the grass and sat down, licking my ice cream, watching the scene before me.

Sunday afternoon at Browns Bay. The world was at peace.

This has become my Sunday ritual. A simple pleasure — ice cream, the beach, the sun. After a week of work and worry, this is my reward. No phone calls, no emails, no urgent matters. Just the sea, the sky, and the sound of children playing.

I thought of the Sundays of my Beijing years — traffice jams, crowded restaurants, endless social obligations. How different this was. How much simpler.

A seagull landed nearby and eyed my ice cream cone with interest. I broke off a piece and tossed it. The seagull caught it mid-air and flew off, followed by a few of its companions.

This, too, is part of the Sunday ritual.

*"When he came, there was no light. When he left, there was no darkness left behind."*

It was a Sunday at the end of June, 2016. Our days in Vanuatu were numbered. Before leaving this beautiful South Pacific island nation, we made plans with our friends from Suzhou, the Weis, for one last island road trip.

A "circumnavigation" of the island was really just a few hours' leisure. The main island's ring road stretched only 120 kilometers — even with stops, a morning was plenty. We set out from Port Vila, drove east past the Second Lagoon, through Banana Bay and its white sand beaches, and by the time we'd reached the midpoint of the drive, we arrived at the Emua wharf.

The wharf was nothing more than a simple jetty. We parked the car and stepped out to enjoy the cool sea breeze. Standing on the jetty, looking out to where the sea met the sky, the silhouette of an island emerged — Nguna.

Just as we were gazing toward the island, a local boatman preparing to launch called out from beside the jetty, warmly asking if we'd like to come aboard. His boat was about to leave.

It was a tiny motorboat, not unlike the pleasure boats in the artificial lake of a park back in my hometown. A small outboard motor hung off the back. Counting the boatman, it could seat six or seven at most. Three locals were already on board. We asked the price — surprisingly cheap. We decided to go with the flow and boarded, curious to explore this unknown island.

The little boat chugged to life, carving a white wake across the calm, amber-blue sea. The warm tropical wind carried salty spray from the bow onto our faces — not irritating, but bearing a kind of equatorial exuberance. Nearby, a flying fish suddenly burst from the placid surface, spreading its wide pectoral fins like wings, gliding gracefully through the air ahead of our boat before plunging back into the sea with a splash.

As we drew closer to Nguna, I looked down. Beneath the crystal-clear, shallow blue water lay giant clams — some as round as wagon wheels, others as large as dining tables — their斑斓 colors shimmering through the waves.

The local passengers, accustomed to these sights, showed little interest. Instead, they cast curious glances at us, these foreigners who looked so different.

And I was equally curious about them. Not because of the color of their skin, but because every one of them — passengers and boatman alike — wore a strikingly conspicuous brown-black T-shirt. On both front and back was printed a large yellow number — "140." Their matching outfits made it seem as though they were heading to some solemn ceremony.

My curiosity got the better of me. Pointing at the number, I asked one of the local men — a sturdy, honest-looking fellow in his mid-twenties — "Are you all going to the island for some special event?"

He smiled and told me they were all natives of Nguna, working on the main island of Efate during the week. Every Sunday, they took the boat back to the island's church for worship.

As for the matching T-shirts with the number, he said solemnly: "It's to commemorate the 140th anniversary of the first English missionary to come to our island."

In early July 2016, we said goodbye to the tropical island and landed in New Zealand, starting a new life on Auckland's North Shore.

New Zealand was breathtakingly beautiful, but life as a new immigrant was far from smooth. Job hunting, renting a house, dealing with the tedious immigration paperwork — time passed in a blur of busy days and anxious nights.

Yet in those weary foreign nights, the number "140" kept floating back into my mind. I couldn't stop thinking about the missionary who had come to Nguna 140 years before.

Who was he? Why did he leave his homeland for a remote place like Nguna? In an age without proper navigational charts, how did he cross the ocean? And what did he experience on that island, still primitive and savage at the time?

Even 140 years later, with all our modern aviation technology, flying from China to Vanuatu was still an exhausting, bumpy ordeal. The thought of traveling there alone in that era was almost unimaginable.

Filled with wonder and questions, I turned to the internet, piecing together the thrilling answer from the dusty pages of history.

---

The missionary who came to Nguna was not English — he was Scottish. His name was Peter Milne.

He was born in Aberdeenshire in northeastern Scotland — a land of towering sea cliffs where the cold gray-blue North Sea pounds the shores day and night, sending up clouds of white spray; a land of the magnificent Cairngorms, where in summer the hillsides blaze with purple heather and in winter lie buried in snow and ice. Peter studied at the historic University of Aberdeen and later at a theological seminary. With his education and talents, he could easily have secured a respectable position in Scotland or England, spending a comfortable life by a warm fireplace with fine cups of tea — the安稳 life most people envy.

But he resolutely chose another path.

In 1870, Peter Milne was sent by the Presbyterian Church of Otago, New Zealand, to missionary work on the island of Nguna in Vanuatu.

What a perilous time it was! Just thirty years before Peter set out for Vanuatu (in 1839), John Williams — known as the "Apostle of the Pacific" — had barely stepped off his boat on the island of Erromango in Vanuatu when he was clubbed to death by enraged native islanders before he could even speak a word. His body was then cannibalized.

And Nguna in 1870 was no less hellish. With European slave traders rampaging across the South Pacific, kidnapping islanders like cattle and selling them as laborers in Fiji, the people of Nguna harbored extreme hostility and hatred toward all white men. On top of that, inter-tribal warfare raged year-round — the victors roasted and ate the bodies of the vanquished. It was the brutal law of survival there.

Around the same time as Peter, another missionary bound for Vanuatu, John Paton, had a famous exchange before his departure.

An elder of the church tried desperately to dissuade him, warning: "You will be eaten by cannibals!"

Paton's eyes blazed as he replied calmly:
*"Mr. Dickson, you are advanced in years now, and your own prospect is soon to be laid in the grave, there to be eaten by worms; I confess to you, that if I can but live and die serving and honoring the Lord Jesus, it will make no difference to me whether I am eaten by cannibals or by worms."*

I often close my eyes and try to picture that chilling scene in my mind:

A young man raised in Western civilized society, highly educated, who could have embraced a middle-class life of comfort — giving up everything. He spent months on a tossing sailing ship, surviving countless dangers, to reach a remote, savage cannibal island cut off from the world.

No solid brick houses with warm fireplaces — only thatched huts that leaked everywhere when the rain poured down. No sizzling Angus steaks fried in butter — only roasted taro and cassava from earth ovens, with their earthy smell. No polite tipping of hats among neighbors and friends — only poisoned spears and bone knives that could fly at you from the shadows at any moment.

Even living more than a century later, as a middle-aged man who has struggled through the hardships of life, I still cannot fathom with ordinary logic — what kind of immense spiritual power could sustain a person in making such a "counter-human" life choice?

Peter Milne put down roots in that harsh environment. He and his wife Mary bound their fate to Nguna Island completely. They stayed — for 54 years. Half a century, and he never left this lonely island, until he died there with white hair.

The historical records of their early sufferings on the island are often brief and vague. Rumors say hostile tribes threw roasted human flesh onto the threshold of their hut to terrorize them. Records show that sharp spears whistled past their ears more than once, nearly killing them. And at least once, they were besieged by hundreds of armed tribal warriors, forced to drive thick stakes into the ground outside their dwelling overnight, building a defensive wall, spending the long night in fear and prayer.

They did not run. They survived, with a posture of extreme humility, carefulness, and yet unbreakable resilience.

They used the modern medical knowledge they brought to treat tribespeople with ulcers and high fevers. Peter, with his astonishing gift for languages, created a written system for the local language, which until then had only been spoken. He compiled the first local dictionary, taught the islanders to read and write, and spent half his life translating the Bible into the local tongue.

He sowed the gospel of God's love and forgiveness, word by word, into soil soaked with hatred.

Under Peter's half-century of cultivation, a miracle truly happened.

Local tribes laid down their bloodstained spears, ended their generation-spanning feuds, and abandoned the horrifying practice of cannibalism. They began to believe in God — to believe that in this world full of killing, there truly existed a selfless love.

There is a stunning epitaph for Peter's life in the historical records:

*"When he came, there was no light. When he left, there was no darkness."*

Peter's family, through multiple generations of flesh and blood, forcibly changed the entire destiny of Nguna Island. And the people of Nguna have not forgotten the stranger who brought them light.

No matter how times change, no matter how the world turns, every ten years, Nguna holds a grand commemorative ceremony to honor the Milne family's arrival on the island. On that day, the entire population of the island — even those who have long since emigrated overseas — travel thousands of miles across the ocean to return.

They put on their T-shirts printed with the year's number, gather in the old church that Peter built with his own hands brick by brick, and sing hymns, commemorating the Scottish elder who brought them the gospel, peace, and civilization.

Today, as I try to type this brief outline of a few hundred words, I feel a deep frustration.

Words are so thin, so powerless. How can these few hundred characters measure the real, tangible fifty-four years — fifty-four years of mosquito bites, rampant disease, loneliness, despair, and countless narrow escapes from death?

I have a habit when reading history — I like to put myself in the shoes of those who came before, to experience what they might have felt. Yet when facing Peter Milne's story, my ability to "put myself in his shoes" completely failed.

It's not because his life was too uneventful, but precisely the opposite — the extreme suffering and extreme devotion he experienced far exceeded the "maximum threshold" of what I, as an ordinary worldly person, can perceive and empathize with.

In other words, it's like a seven-year-old child trying to truly understand the weight carried by a middle-aged man burdened with a mortgage, supporting elderly parents, and fighting in the arena of名利.

Though I cannot truly "feel" what Peter felt, my trembling soul knows clearly: that was a weight I could not bear even if I were cut into a thousand pieces. That kind of pure, self-sacrificial choice — made for his faith — had already transcended the entire "worldview system" I had built over fifty years for weighing worldly gains and losses.

---

And as I trace the thread of history, I find that Peter Milne was not alone in his era. Alongside those who ventured into the Pacific like John Williams and John Paton, a group of equally radiant figures stood in ancient, suffering China.

I think of the young Englishman named Hudson Taylor, who left behind a moving vow:
*"If I had a thousand lives, I would give them all for China."*

He was not just shouting slogans. He buried himself, his wife, and even his young children — their lives forever laid in the soil of China.

I think of Samuel Pollard, who gave his entire life to the Miao people in Shimenkan, Guizhou, dying of typhus and buried in that barren, mountainous land.

I think of Eric Liddell — the Olympic champion, the Scottish Flying Scotsman — who abandoned a brilliant future, gave his life to China, and died tragically in a Japanese internment camp in Weifang, Shandong.

I think of Gladys Aylward — that remarkable woman who, amid the flames of war, led a hundred Chinese orphans across the Taihang Mountains, through countless dangers, to safety in Xi'an.

I think of James O. Fraser, who ventured alone into the Nu River Gorge in Yunnan, creating a written script for the Lisu people, spreading the gospel, and eventually dying of illness in Baoshan, Yunnan, worn out by his labors.

In the long history of missionary work, there are so many more — countless names that have not even been recorded. They abandoned the comfort, wealth, and fame that ordinary people spend their lives pursuing. They burned their one and only life, without reservation, in the darkness of foreign lands.

I cannot bear to simply list their names lightly on this cold screen. Because the experience of any one of them is enough to fill a weighty, epic life story — one to be sung with tears.

Each of them was not only a monument of humanity that transcended borders and races, but also a flame that never extinguishes. This flame illuminated the darkest corners of this cruel world and allowed countless people like me — who have sunk in the mundane — to stop in their hurried steps, let go of the worldly calculations clutched tightly in their hands, and look up to see a true and eternal path.

Today is Friday, April 3, 2026.

In New Zealand, it is a most sacred public holiday, and the peace of the holiday will extend until the coming Monday.

It is not just a long weekend for leisure. This day is Good Friday — in history, it was called "Black Friday."

To be honest, I have always known very little about Christianity. That thick Bible — I have read it only sporadically, grasping only the surface.

I first approached the Bible largely under my mother's influence. Later, as my life experience deepened — seeing too much absurdity in this world, too many partings between life and death, too much of nature's irresistible power — I began, at the edge of reason, to try to believe in the existence of a higher-dimensional God.

But I must admit, my "faith" is not only shallow, but filled with worldly calculations.

When life strikes a heavy blow and I sink into the "quiet void" of depression and defeat, I privately nurse resentment in my heart, accusing the invisible God: Why are you so unfair to me? Why have you taken away everything I worked so hard for?

And when the storm passes, when life goes smoothly and problems are solved, I attribute it all to my own endurance, intelligence, and outstanding ability. My proud self-esteem whispers secretly: Since I can handle everything myself, why should I bow down to follow a God I cannot see or touch?

This is me — an ordinary man trapped in the shackles of materialism and personal pride, who has spent half his life fighting for "dignity" and a "square meter of self."

Yet, on this solemn Black Friday, as that old missionary buried on a desolate South Pacific island — and those figures who left their blood and bones on the Taihang Mountains and the Yunnan-Guizhou plateau — surge toward me like a tidal wave, my pitiful pride and complaints are instantly shattered into dust.

If God does not exist, if everything is merely a transactional exchange for worldly benefit —

Then who gave these carefree middle-class youths such insane courage, that they could smile as they walked into the lands of cannibals?

Who gave them the strength, in the face of spears and malaria, to still love with their whole lives those who once sought to kill them?

In that moment, on this cool early autumn morning, it seemed I finally touched the edge of an answer.

Not worldly gain or loss. Not a transaction for the afterlife. But that they truly, in their rough mortal bodies, lived out the likeness of the carpenter's son who was nailed to a cross on that "Black Friday" two thousand years ago.

These missionaries who came one after another — with their magnificent yet profoundly quiet lives — have delivered the most震撼 spiritual verdict to me, a middle-aged man who has haggled over every worldly gain for half a century —

They offered their own lives as a sacrifice to tell me clearly: The God who rules in the darkness does not only truly exist; more than that, God's highest law is not to grant you wealth and glory in this life, but through a love that transcends life and death — called "redemption" — to let you, in this brief, decaying material world, finally glimpse the only imperishable, eternal life.

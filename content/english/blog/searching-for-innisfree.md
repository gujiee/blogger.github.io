*"These walls are funny. First you hate them, then you get used to them. Enough time passes, you get so you depend on them. That's institutionalized."*

For years, I have asked myself the same question: Where is my hometown? Ningbo? Changzhi? Or Beijing?

On my Chinese residence registration, the "ancestral home" column reads: Dinghai, Ningbo. The Jiangnan region, prosperous since ancient times — *"Emerald waters reaching the sky, painted boats listening to the rain"* or *"At sunrise, river flowers burn redder than fire; in spring, the river flows green as indigo"* — such beautiful words, such beautiful scenes. I ought to feel some longing for it. But honestly, I have never been there. I have left no trace there. Even my father's memories of the place are extremely faint. He traveled all over the country with my grandfather from a young age; all that remains of his hometown is a southern face and a few half-remembered phrases of dialect. In my subconscious, Ningbo is merely a beautiful, hollow mark on paper.

The place I was truly born and raised is a small city in Shanxi — Changzhi. The Clear Zhang River and the Muddy Zhang River flow through it, rushing down the rugged Taihang Mountains all the way to Cao Cao's Bronze Sparrow Platform. There are the endless Taihang ranges, deep gorges, and dense forests. From birth until university, I spent a full eighteen years there. There were my teachers and classmates; the slender white poplars standing straight as brush handles outside my window; the white clouds drifting lazily overhead. My accent still carries its traces.

By all logic, this should be my hometown. Yet I rarely miss those mountains and waters, and I don't deeply yearn for the people there. I only remember that during the New Year, the price of steamed buns would multiply several times over. I remember that there, whether for schooling or medical treatment, you had to know someone, pull some strings. The whole small city was like an enormous, airtight web of relationships — and I was never at the center of that web.

---

So for a very long time, I stubbornly considered Beijing my hometown.

Ever since I was a child, my mother painted pictures of Beijing for me. There was the railway elementary school she attended, with its dedicated teachers. There were little boats drifting across Beihai Lake, steaming lamb buns served near the palace gates, bright red candied hawthorns at Baitasi (the White Pagoda Temple), and seasonal snacks sold by street vendors peddling through the alleys.

Before I went to Beijing for university, my mother took me during holidays to visit my grandmother's home near the White Pagoda Temple. It was a quintessential Beijing *siheyuan* — a traditional courtyard house with a Chinese scholar tree, a horse-tooth jujube tree, and a walnut tree. The communal tap in the center of the courtyard dispensed cold, sweet groundwater — even in the oppressive heat of summer, it remained bone-chillingly cool. Through the rustling leaves of the locust tree, the towering white pagoda was visible. When the breeze blew, the copper bells on the pagoda's crown rang out with a melodious, otherworldly chime.

When I filled out my college entrance application, I unhesitatingly marked every option as a school in Beijing. I got my wish, and like a single drop of water finally flowing into a great river, I arrived. From then on, I lived in Beijing for twenty years — longer than my birthplace of Changzhi.

Here I made lifelong friends and built a stable life. Here I married, had a child, started a company, and bought my own home. Here I welcomed my lovely daughter, Pangbao. I grew accustomed to drinking tea at Fragrant Hills, strolling by Houhai, listening to Buddhist chants at Guangji Temple, and seeking out snacks in Niujie Street. Deep in my soul, I had already made Beijing my hometown.

I dreamed countless times of living out the rest of my days here in happiness: caring for my parents, loving my wife, raising Pangbao to adulthood, growing old myself. Yes, I had encountered bureaucratic harassment, tax fines, local thugs, and arrogant officials — but I dismissed these as part of life's normal texture and never really took them to heart.

I loved this city deeply. I naively believed it loved me too.

---

Until 2013.

I had lived in my apartment for five years by then. The neighbors were friendly, life was convenient. A large supermarket had just opened across from the complex. To the west, separated by just one road, was the branch campus of a prestigious local elementary school. Everything seemed to be moving in the right direction.

Then disaster struck, without warning.

A twelve-year-old boy from upstairs, on his way to school at noon, was run over by an engineering vehicle belonging to a giant state-owned enterprise at the entrance of our complex. He fell into a deep coma — a vegetative state. In the days that followed, his parents withered rapidly, hollow with grief. Every time I ran into them in the complex, I couldn't bring myself to say hello, afraid that any glib word of comfort would only tear open their wounds again. The residents organized a donation for the child in the plaza. The sum wasn't much, but we hoped it might offer the parents some small comfort.

Six months later, the child passed away.

And that deep-pocketed state-owned enterprise — not only did it fail to step up, take responsibility, and offer fair compensation — it revealed an appalling cold-blooded ruthlessness. They pulled strings to erase the skid marks from the road. They destroyed all surveillance footage from the surrounding area. They even turned around and blamed the deceased minor, swearing up and down that "the child ran into the vehicle himself."

Later, after an arduous struggle, the child's parents finally found the truth on a hidden camera behind a kindergarten tree. The footage clearly showed the child pushing a cart when the engineering vehicle struck him from behind.

Confronted with irrefutable evidence, the state-owned enterprise changed its story again, arguing shamelessly that a illegally parked private car had blocked the bus, which in turn blocked the engineering vehicle's line of sight — so the primary fault lay with the private car.

I don't know much about the law. But I witnessed with my own eyes how human integrity could be twisted and turned upside down.

The giant state-owned enterprise won the lawsuit, as expected.

And I — while praying for those poor parents — suddenly felt a bone-deep chill. I looked at myself with sadness and realized: if it had been me, I would have been just as powerless. I could have done nothing, changed nothing, except bear the immense injustice in silence.

It was in that moment that I truly understood the weight of the term *caomin* — "grass people" — as light as a feather. For the first time, I felt a deep fear of this city I loved, and of my future life.

---

That fear reached its peak in 2014.

My precious Pangbao was growing up, approaching school age. I wasn't asking for an exorbitantly priced school-district apartment or a top-tier名校. My only wish was for her to attend the school just across the road from our complex — so that I could watch her cross the street safely every day. I took it for granted that this was only natural.

But "wishes," in this land, are often the cheapest currency.

When policies changed as capriciously as shifting clouds, I initially clung to the naive belief that there would be a bottom line. Until I went through one absurd condition after another, one impossibly convoluted verification after another. Until I joined countless desperate parents and spent a full day and night waiting outside the school's locked gates. Until dawn broke and we were met with the school's icy reply: "All spots are filled. Find your own solution."

My heart was completely shattered. Frozen.

I could not understand how a vast administrative machine could so blatantly tear up its own contracts, so arrogantly trample the dignity of ordinary people underfoot. But it was happening. It happened before my eyes. It happened to me. It happened to countless Chinese citizens like me, who have no privileges, no power.

I looked at my young daughter and could not imagine her future path on this soil: middle school, high school, university, finding a job... Every threshold in life lay not in her own efforts, nor under my protection. Above this sky, there would always be an enormous invisible hand,暗中 manipulating the abacus of power. Every rule of the game here revolves around connections, background, and money.

This is not the hometown I want. This is not soil where I can entrust my daughter's future in peace.

---

In those suffocating, powerless nights, Yeats' poem echoed through my mind again and again:

*I will arise and go now, and go to Innisfree,*
*And a small cabin build there, of clay and wattles made;*
*Nine bean rows will I have there, a hive for the honey bee,*
*And live alone in the bee-loud glade.*

I know that in every person's heart, there is a small island. On that island, there is azure water, swaying palm trees, a gentle breeze — and a dignity that will no longer be casually trampled by power. And nine rows of bean sprouts, all your own, in perfect peace.

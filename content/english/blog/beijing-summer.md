Summer in Beijing arrives abruptly — not because it comes quickly, but because spring leaves too suddenly. When people are still immersed in the warm weather and the riot of colors, spring, like a mischievous child, runs off without a word. Only the observant can find traces of its departure in the color of leaves and the scattered fallen petals.

And then summer is here. When speaking of Beijing summer, one must mention the cool shade of Guangji Temple and the night scenery of Shichahai.

Guangji Temple is at the Xisi intersection, opposite the Geological Museum — now the Ministry of Land and Resources. There used to be a Xinhua Bookstore at the Xisi intersection; it is still there, though I am not sure if it still operates. The Xisi station of Subway Line 4 is right next to the temple — about two hundred meters from the exit. If you come north from Xidan, you can stroll all the way. It is not far. After browsing Zhongyou Department Store and Xidan Market, after watching the bustling crowds of all kinds of people, you need a place to rest your feet — and your mind. Guangji Temple is a fine choice.

Guangji Temple follows the classic three-courtyard structure. The mountain gate is modest, unremarkable gray brick and tile, with the character "佛" (Buddha) adorned in colored glaze beside the entrance. Step through the gate, and a wave of coolness instantly envelops you.

In the temple, the old locust trees are ancient and sprawling, their branches creating a dense canopy that blocks out the summer sun. The eaves of the main hall rise gracefully, their corners upturned against the blue sky. In the courtyard, the incense burner sends up wisps of blue smoke. The scent of sandalwood mingles with the cool shade.

I once came here in early summer. I sat on the stone steps of the main hall, watching an elderly monk sweep the courtyard. He moved slowly and deliberately, each stroke of the broom tracing a arc on the bluestone floor. The leaves that had fallen from the locust trees were gathered into small piles, then swept into a dustpan. The monk's face was calm, his eyes clear, as if nothing in the world could disturb him.

I thought of the noisy streets outside — the traffic, the crowds, the endless hustle. And I thought of this quiet courtyard where time seemed to stand still. The monk finished sweeping and carried the dustpan toward the back. Before disappearing through a side door, he turned and nodded at me. I nodded back.

That nod — no words were needed. In that moment, I understood that the cool shade of Guangji Temple was not just the shade of locust trees. It was a coolness of the heart.

After leaving the temple, I walked toward Shichahai. The sun was setting, and the lake was covered with golden light. The willow trees along the shore swayed in the evening breeze. Couples sat on benches, boats drifted on the water, and the sounds of erhu music drifted from a distant courtyard.

This is Beijing summer. The noisy and the quiet, the bustling and the still — all coexist in the same city, under the same sky. You can choose. You can be among the crowds at Xidan, or you can be alone in the temple courtyard. Both are Beijing. Both are summer.

The afternoon was quiet. The street outside had been so busy just days before — cars and people streaming in both directions — but now there was almost no one. The shops along the road were closed one by one, their doors hung with red couplets, gold characters shining against the crimson paper. The sound of firecrackers came from time to time, muffled and distant, as if the whole world had slowed down.

I sat on the deck, looking out at the sea. The tide was low, and the exposed sand stretched far into the distance, the surface rippled by the retreating water. A few seagulls stood on the wet sand, occasionally lifting their heads to look around, then lowering them again to peck at something.

The sun hung low in the western sky, a golden disc about to touch the horizon. The clouds around it were edged with red, layer upon layer, like a fired clay sculpture. The sea reflected the sky's colors, turning the water into a vast expanse of shimmering gold.

I thought of the New Year's Eves I had spent in Beijing — the crowded streets, the endless traffic, the noisy gatherings, the forced cheerfulness. I thought of the long-distance calls I used to make, the repeated New Year's wishes, the tiredness that followed. And I thought of this year — this quiet, peaceful afternoon, sitting on the deck, watching the sea, listening to the gulls.

The year before this one had not been easy. There had been so much running around, so much worry, so many sleepless nights. But at this moment, all of it seemed far away, like something that had happened to another person. I wondered if this was what growing older felt like — not the physical aging, but the gradual realization that the things that once seemed so urgent were not so urgent after all.

My daughter came out onto the deck, holding a cup of tea for me. She had grown taller over the past year, and there was something different in her face — a quietness that had not been there before. She sat down beside me without saying anything, and we watched the sunset together.

"This is nice," she said after a long while.

"Yes," I said. "This is nice."

The sun dipped below the horizon, and the sky turned from gold to purple to dark blue. A few stars appeared, faint at first, then growing brighter. The tide was coming in now, covering the wet sand, inch by inch. The gulls had flown away, and the world grew even quieter.

I thought of the word "home" — what it meant, where it was. For so many years, I had thought of home as Beijing. But now, sitting on this deck with my daughter, watching the sun set over the sea, I was not so sure. Perhaps home was not a place but a moment — a moment of stillness, of connection, of being exactly where you were meant to be.

The New Year had begun, quietly, without fanfare. And I was grateful.

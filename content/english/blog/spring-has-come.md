Every spring, I have the same dream. In the dream, there is an old school — very small, with classrooms surrounding a courtyard. The classrooms are made of green brick and gray tile. Inside, there are young students, following their teacher in loud recitation. Outside the classroom window is a small pond, ringed with willow trees. In spring, the pond water is soft, and the willow branches are soft, too. The tender new buds paint the willows with a faint, yellowish-green glow.

The sun is bright today, the air is warm. The classroom doors and windows are open, and through them you can see the soft willow branches swaying in the breeze. I must be among the students — yes, I am sitting in the front row, reciting loudly along with the teacher: *Spring Has Come*.

> Spring has come, spring has come,
> to the little stream —
> the stream flows merrily.

> Spring has come, spring has come,
> to the fields —
> the grass is green, the flowers are blooming.

> Spring has come, spring has come,
> to the birds' home —
> the birds sing beautiful songs.

> Spring has come, spring has come,
> to the school —
> the school is filled with laughter and joy.

As I read, I seem to leave my seat, floating up like a kite. I see the classroom in the sunlight, the children reciting loudly. I see the pond shimmering in the sunlight. I see the tender willow buds swaying in the breeze. I float higher and higher, until the voices of the children grow faint, and the willow buds are no longer clear. Only the pond remains, bright as a mirror beneath the sun. I look into the mirror, trying to see my reflection — but I see a man in a suit and glasses. It is me, as I am now.

I have had this dream many times. Each time I wake, I feel something missing in my heart, as if I have lost something. Was it that old school? The pond with its willow trees? Or the bare, earthy ground beside the pond in spring — so close, so intimate? I do not know. Even now, I do not know.

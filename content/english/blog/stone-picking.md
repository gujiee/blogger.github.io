I half-crouched, carefully examining every pebble that lay quietly on the riverbank. I was searching for the legendary "Tao River gem" — a dark green stone with a smooth surface, hard and dense, with translucent patterns running through it. This stone is found only in Zhuoma County, in the upper reaches of the Tao River, on the steep cliffs lining both banks. Since ancient times, agile Tibetan collectors, ropes tied around their waists, have descended these cliffs to mine Tao River stone. Master carvers would then shape it into various objects, treasured by the powerful through the ages. Scholars would make inkstones from it — the stone was so smooth that ink would not dry overnight. These Tao inkstones became famous throughout China as one of the four great inkstones.

Of the stones used for inkstones, mountain stone is inferior; water stone is best. "Water stone" refers to stones that have fallen into the Tao River, soaked and polished by the current for millions of years. Their surfaces are smoother, their internal patterns more translucent, often revealing different images. But they are rare and hard to find. These are the true "Tao River gems."

In truth, every pebble on the riverbank is beautiful. They come in many colors — dark red,藏蓝, ochre, earthy yellow, indigo, white. Polished by the Tao River year after year, their surfaces are smooth and fine, with all kinds of纹理. Under the highland sun, every stone gives off a dazzling light. They are unlike the pebbles commonly seen in cities — covered in dust, stained with grease, piled with garbage, with no flowing water or fresh breeze to accompany them, reduced like cement and brick to the mundane stuff of the world.

I picked up a pebble and examined it closely, feeling its translucent smoothness. It was hard to believe it was a lifeless little stone. It must have its story — a story so complex that recounting it would fill more pages than a great encyclopedia.

How many years had it existed? Though it was so delicate, it must be at least hundreds of millions of years old. A hundred years is the span of a human life, but to this stone, it is but a blink. A thousand years, with all the bloodshed and dynastic changes of human history, is to it a mere snap of the fingers. Five thousand years, the entire span of human civilization, is to it but the lifespan of a mayfly.

Where was its home? In the deep ocean? In the distant Andes mountains? Or on the towering peak of Everest?

How did it come to be here? Was it carried by a rushing flood? Ridden a giant tsunami? Or brought by a rare hurricane?

Did it have siblings? How had it passed the endless years? Leaning against a loved one? Sharing the storms with friends? Or alone, listening to the music of the mountains and rivers, struggling bravely in heart-stopping rapids?

Had others, like me, picked it up gently and examined it closely? Who were they? A Han warrior pursuing the Xiongnu westward? A Tang monk seeking the great truth of the world? A Song loyalist exiled to the frontier? Or an innocent child, knowing nothing of the world's affairs?

I thought of Emily Dickinson's poem:

> How happy is the little Stone
> That rambles in the Road alone,
> And does not care about careers,
> And Exigencies never fears —
> Whose coat of elemental Brown
> A passing Universe put on,
> And independent as the Sun,
> Associates or glows alone,
> Fulfilling absolute Decree
> In casual simplicity —

How happy to be a little stone.

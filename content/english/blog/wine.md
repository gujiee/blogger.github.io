The weather was strange — spring should have been near, but a cold, gloomy rain swept in instead. The sweater I had taken off was not yet washed, and I had to put it back on.

That evening, I picked up Pangbao and got home around 7:30. I gave her some orange juice and played with her for a while. She was getting sleepy. I changed her clothes and put her to bed. It was nearly 8:30.

I gently closed the bedroom door. The chill in the living room made me shiver. I checked the thermometer — only 15 degrees. I put on a jacket and felt warmer, but then I realized I was hungry. I went to the kitchen. The freezer was almost out of dumplings, and there was nothing else I could eat right away. I thought about making some congee, but that would take too long, so I gave up.

On the dining table was a bag of peanuts, bought during the New Year and not yet finished. I grabbed it, sat on the sofa, and ate a couple. They lacked something. I fetched a bottle of highland barley liquor from the bookcase — it had been sitting there for two years. A friend gave me six bottles in 2006, when I was in Qinghai. I gave two to my father-in-law, who loved his drink. I gave two to my former political commissar He — we used to stay up late chatting and drinking, sometimes until dawn. I kept two for myself. One I opened in October 2007 for Pangbao's one-month celebration. The other I had been drinking, on and off, for two years — it was still almost full.

I love the flavor of highland barley liquor. It is light and fragrant, not strong — you only smell it when you bring the bottle close. The liquor is clear and pure, like the air of a high-plateau autumn day. When it touches your lips, it is slightly sharp at first, then sweet and mellow, lingering long after.

I filled my glass and took a deep sip. As the warmth spread through my body, I felt much better. Chewing peanuts, sipping highland barley liquor — on that cold, hungry spring night, it brought me unexpected comfort.

Soon, before I had finished one glass, I was already tipsy. Gentle ripples of pleasure spread slowly from deep within me, growing stronger. It was a feeling of complete release — unbound, free, floating between heaven and earth. No more troubling trifles, no more worries about the future.

Wine truly is a wonderful thing. Otherwise, why would Ruan Ji travel with a wine jug, drinking as he went? Why would Li Bai invite the moon to drink with him, raising his cup and singing loudly? Why would Li Qingzhao row home late, tipsy, straying deep into the lotus grove? Why would Su Shi lament that life is but a dream, and toast the river moon?

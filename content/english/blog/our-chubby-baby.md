I might be going to Qinghai tomorrow. This trip was not planned — I had only meant to stay in Zhengzhou for two days before returning to Beijing. But now I must head west, farther and farther from home. I did not bring enough luggage for a long trip, not enough clothes. Everything is so rushed.

After dinner, I returned to my hotel room and turned on the TV. Without thinking, I switched to Channel 1. At home, at this time, I would just be finishing dinner too. I would call out, "Pangbao! Come watch TV with Daddy!" And Pangbao would come running, holding her favorite Mickey Mouse doll, toddling happily behind me. If I pretended to run away, she would quicken her pace, stumbling a little. Then I would spin around, crouch down, and open my arms. Pangbao would stop, look at me, as if considering whether to turn and run — but then she would run toward me, her little arms open wide, throw herself into my arms, and squeal with pure joy.

I would hold her, patting her gently. She would press her soft little cheek against mine, reach one tiny arm under my armpit, and pat my back the same way I patted hers.

I would turn the TV to Channel 1, where her favorite show, Big Windmill, was on. She would stand by my legs, one hand on the coffee table, watching TV while tapping her music box and swaying her little body to the rhythm.

Around 8 o'clock, it was Pangbao's bedtime. I would wash her face and feet, change her into her pajamas. She would drink a bottle of milk, snuggled in my arms. Then I would carry her around the bedroom, reciting Tang poems and Song lyrics, until she began to snore softly, and I would lay her in her crib.

Ever since she was born, Pangbao liked to sleep on her stomach, her face turned to one side, her little arms at her sides. Her face, pressed against the sheet by her own weight, looked even fuller, her lips pursed adorably, her long eyelashes resting against her cheeks like a cartoon character. I often knelt by the crib, watching her as she fell asleep, her milky breath so soft and tender.

She would wake me in the night — sometimes with a tiny hand patting my face, sometimes with her soft little mouth kissing my cheek. She would point to her bottle and say, very solemnly, "Milk." Even though I was still drifting in a sweet dream, I had to satisfy her demand, or she would fight me to the end.

In the morning, I no longer needed an alarm clock. Pangbao always woke me before it rang — 4:30, 5:00. If I managed to sleep until 6:00, I was very lucky. After feeding her, I would sometimes try to stay in bed a little longer, but she would sing songs in her own language and pat my body until I could not bear it anymore and had to get up.

When she was around, life always felt chaotic. When she was not, I missed her endlessly.

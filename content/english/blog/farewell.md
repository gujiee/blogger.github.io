In early September in Qinghai, the wind already carried a biting edge. The project in Xining had finally come to an end. It had been a long, somewhat monotonous few months — shuttling between the Agriculture and Animal Husbandry Building on Shengli Road and the factory in the Chenbei Biological Park, installing equipment on the smooth cement floors of the new workshop, supervising the connection of pipes, valves, and control systems. Every night I pored over instruction manuals, preparing for the next day's work. When the equipment trial run succeeded, I felt a euphoric release.

I packed my bags and asked the front desk to book a ticket back to Beijing. My plan was simple: sleep for three days straight, then go to Hongyuan Hotpot at the south gate of Tiantan, order a plate of quick-fried tripe, grab a bottle of iced Yanjing beer, and wash away the months of accumulated high-plateau dust and chill.

But life always likes to throw a curveball. The afternoon before I was set to leave for the airport, the company called.

"Xiao Gu, the Xining project is finished? Everything went smoothly? Good, good — hard work," my boss said, his voice on the other end conspicuously warm. That was never a good sign. "If it's convenient on your way back, make a detour to Lintao in Gansu. Xingda Industrial Group has some minor equipment issues — go take a look, make some adjustments. Shouldn't take more than a few days."

I hung up and smiled bitterly. A detour? On the map, that distance was a finger-width. In reality, it was hundreds of kilometers. But it was work; it paid the bills. Complaints aside, I had to change my plans. I cancelled my ticket and replanned my route. Xingda's factory was in Lintao County, Gansu — not far from Lanzhou, about a hundred kilometers due south.

---

But before I could get to Lanzhou, I had to figure out how to get from Xining to Lanzhou.

There were options — buses, trains. But I had a particular fondness for the intercity double-decker express train, the "Shenzhou," running between Xining and Lanzhou. I always bought a ticket for the upper deck. Fewer people, more space, no noisy chatter — and most importantly, the view. From the high upper deck, you could see over the railway fencing without obstruction: the year-round snow on the distant Qilian Mountains, the eternal shimmer of the Datong River, and the cattle and sheep grazing leisurely on the grasslands. I would bring a copy of *Reader* or *One Hundred Years of Solitude* — read a few pages in the rocking train, gaze at the scenery for a while. The three-hour journey became a rare pleasure.

---

That morning, I bought my ticket at Xining Station, familiar as ever. Sunlight streamed through the station's tall glass dome onto the floor. I held the pale pink ticket, followed the crowd through the gate, and stepped onto the platform. The white double-decker train sat quietly on the tracks — stylish and luxurious compared to the dark green single-deckers around it.

I found my carriage, one near the front. Following my usual habit, I walked briskly toward the staircase leading to the upper deck. I was already looking forward to picking a window seat, stowing my backpack, and settling comfortably into my seat.

But just as my foot touched the first step, a white-gloved hand appeared, blocking my way.

"Comrade, you can't go up there."

The hand belonged to a young train attendant. She wore a crisp uniform, her hair meticulously pinned up. Her face bore light makeup, but her expression was as cold as the frost of a Xining winter. Her tone was less a suggestion than an order — a refusal delivered with an air of unassailable superiority.

"Why not? My ticket is..." I instinctively began to argue, holding up my ticket.

"No reason. We received notice — the upper deck of this carriage has been requisitioned. Please take a seat downstairs. There are plenty of empty seats down there." She didn't look at my ticket. She didn't even look at my eyes. Her gaze passed over my shoulder, fixed on the carriage door, as if some important personage was about to appear at any moment.

I paused and looked up toward the upper deck. It was completely empty. Sunlight streamed through the windows onto the blue velvet seats. Dust danced in the light beams — an unreal stillness.

I'm not one to make a fuss, especially when traveling. I shrugged, withdrew my foot, and turned into the lower deck.

A few scattered passengers sat in the lower carriage. I chose a window seat, stowed my backpack in the overhead rack. I was annoyed, but helpless. That's life — you never know what rules you'll encounter next, or what "notice" will change your plans.

---

Before long, a rapid, synchronized footstep approached from the carriage connection. I turned my head. At the staircase I had just tried to climb, two young police officers now stood. They wore brimmed hats, white duty belts around their waists, and their sharp eyes swept the surroundings like a pair of stern gatekeepers, sealing off the upper deck entirely. An invisible pressure instantly filled the cramped space. The whispered conversations in the lower carriage dimmed, as if someone had pressed a mute button. Everyone lowered their voices or fell silent altogether.

Clearly, that "empty world" upstairs had become forbidden territory.

---

Since I couldn't watch the distant scenery, I turned my gaze to the platform outside. Because the lower-deck windows were low, I was at eye level with the platform, and the people on the platform generally didn't look down — they wouldn't notice these eyes behind the glass. This gave me a furtive sense of pleasure, like a secret observer.

About a dozen people suddenly clustered onto the platform in front of my carriage, like a swarm of black beetles.

They were a peculiar group. Even in Xining, a remote provincial capital in the Northwest, they looked remarkably standardized — as if stamped from the same mold. Every one of them was impeccably maintained, with fair skin that, even under the plateau's intense UV rays, showed a kind of rosy, oily sheen cultivated in long-term office comfort. They stood in stark contrast to the ordinary travelers around them — dark-skinned, weathered by wind and frost.

Their attire was strikingly uniform. All dark tones — navy or black jackets, zippers pulled precisely to chest height; crisp dark trousers; black leather shoes polished to a gleam, speckled with platform dust but still reflecting the midday sun. The only differences were in their ages, the density of their hair, and the degree of paunch straining against their belts.

I guessed what they were: a send-off party.

The "big leader" who had requisitioned the entire upper deck must be their target. This farewell ritual had probably begun at dawn outside some luxury hotel — complete with a police escort, a direct drive onto the platform, handshakes and reluctant farewells, culminating in the leader's final, powerful wave before he turned and disappeared into the carriage.

Now the farewell drama had reached its epilogue.

The protagonist had already exited — boarded the train. But the curtain — the departure bell — had not yet fallen. All the supporting actors had to remain on stage, maintaining their performance.

All the requisite pleasantries and courtesies had clearly been exhausted. All the declarations of loyalty and reluctance had presumably been conveyed in that final, firm handshake. Yet no one dared to leave before the departure bell rang.

And so the platform presented a bizarre, exquisitely微妙 "dynamic stillness."

Through this slightly grimy window glass, I watched them like a silent film. Their faces all wore the same carefully maintained, appropriately proportioned smile. Not a genuine laugh — that would seem unserious. Not a sad, bitter smile — that would seem inauspicious. It was a standard "farewell smile" — warm and heartfelt enough, yet not so fawning as to lose dignity. The angle of the upturned lips seemed precisely calculated through long practice: one degree more would be fake, one degree less, cold.

Most of them held a subtle posture: feet slightly apart for endurance, but upper bodies leaning forward toward the carriage. This stance was meticulous — it conveyed a "reluctance to part, a humility that wanted to hear a few more words of instruction even through the steel shell," while also ensuring that if the leader suddenly opened a window or appeared at the door, they could rush forward immediately, without obstruction, to receive instructions.

But awkwardness was spreading through the crowd like an invisible fog.

The big leader had clearly settled in, with no intention of reappearing. So these dozen-odd people standing below, smiling at a steel carriage — the scene was almost farcical. Yet no one dared let the air fall completely silent — that would seem insufficiently "warm," insufficiently "devoted."

Some drew closer, forming three or four small groups. They began carrying on seemingly animated but utterly meaningless conversations — using exaggerated mouth shapes, gestures, even body postures. Pointing at distant buildings, patting each other's shoulders, as if discussing how brilliant some "important instruction" left by the leader had been. But if you watched closely, you could see that their eyes never engaged with the person they were talking to. Their eyes were wandering — like radar. Though they were conversing, their gazes remained locked, not too boldly, on the upper-deck windows.

They tried to penetrate that glass, to catch even a flicker of a "response" from the leader. Maybe a shadow moving, maybe a wave. But the moment they sensed that someone might be looking back from behind those windows, they would swiftly — like startled rabbits — make their eyes even more humble, even more submissive, even flusteredly avoiding direct eye contact, and throw themselves with greater vigor into "heated discussions" with those around them, demonstrating their work ethic and reverence for authority.

---

In this suffocating standoff, one of the men — perhaps lower-ranking or more socially adept — seemed to find the silence unbearable. He fumbled in his jacket pocket and pulled out a pack of Soft China cigarettes — the era's ultimate social currency.

He didn't light one for himself. Instead, he began awkwardly distributing them to those around him. His movements were almost urgent, as if this were a critical mission. One after another, the white cigarettes passed between plump fingers. This wasn't about satisfying a nicotine craving — it was a social prop, a lifeline.

The others seized the cigarettes as if grabbing lifebuoys. Over the next two minutes, the scene came "alive." Lighters flickered, flames were cupped, cigarettes were lit, offered from one to another, deeply inhaled, smoke rings blown... This sequence of actions perfectly filled the dead time, masking their手足无措 discomposure.

The flames flickered in the wind. Smoke rose and curled, illuminating faces full of expectation, anxiety, eagerness to please, and — in the corners of their eyes — a barely perceptible, bone-deep fatigue.

They stood there, bound by an invisible covenant called "power." Each of them performed "warmth" and "loyalty" with all their might, but beneath that warmth lay a barely concealed absurdity. Every one of them was probably thinking: "Why won't this damn train just leave already?" "My legs are killing me." "Did Old Li stand closer to the front than I did?" Each wanted to be the one who stayed until the very last second, proving their loyalty. But at the same time, each was probably desperately hoping the whistle would blow, ending this prolonged ordeal.

---

I sat by the window on the lower deck, watching this absurd yet deeply真实 drama unfold.

I was no different from them, in essence. They were trapped on that tiny platform, held fast by the gravitational field of power, unable to move. And I was trapped in this dim lower-deck carriage, blocked from the upper deck by a staircase called "privilege." We were all supporting actors on this vast stage. The only difference was that they were costumed extras, and I was a silent spectator who had stumbled backstage.

---

Finally, after what felt like an eternity — 

*Woo——*

The long whistle of the departing train pierced the frozen air. The carriage jolted, metal joints clanking, and began to slide slowly forward.

The dozen-odd tense, oily faces — at almost the same instant — their rigid, fake smiles crumbled. Replacing them was the most genuine, most radiant smile I had seen all day — perhaps in my entire life. It was the smile of relief. The lightness of a released prisoner.

In unison, they raised their right arms and began waving vigorously. Their heads turned like精密 radar, tracking the slowly moving upper-deck carriage. Their gazes were no longer wandering — they were fixed with "deep emotion" on that window, behind which perhaps no one was looking at all.

The train accelerated. That tiny platform, those dozen-odd waving hands, that cluster of middle-aged men in dark jackets — receded rapidly, shrinking until they disappeared at the edge of sight. I imagined that now, finally, they could lower their aching arms, stub out those damned cigarettes, climb into their official cars, curse — "What a fucking exhausting day" — and collapse into their seats.

I turned my head and looked at the widening landscape outside the window. The muddy waves of the Huangshui River rolled under the sun. In the distance, the mountains remained silent dust. The upper deck stayed quiet and empty. Downstairs, the sounds of children crying and passengers chatting resumed. The train clattered on, carrying all of us — those above and those below, those performing and those watching — together toward an unknown destination.

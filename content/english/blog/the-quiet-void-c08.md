These days, Gu Yuan was distracted. Keeping such a huge secret to himself, unable to tell anyone or make anyone believe him — the feeling was unbearable. It was a kind of煎熬. He deliberately stayed away from the video room, trying to forget everything that had happened there. But things always work this way: the more you try to forget, the more you remember. And his overwhelming curiosity drove him to open the second video.

The tape was coded 1055. In the classified catalog, it was listed as the "Dulin Farm Incident." He chose it because the tape was dated 1994 — not too far removed from the present compared to the 1947 Roswell Incident and the 1980 Rendlesham Forest Incident. Also, it happened in China. Unlike the vague, hard-to-follow exchanges in the Roswell interviews, Gu Yuan thought Chinese would be easier to understand.

The video began, as before, with the number 7 filling the screen. When the 0 faded, black text appeared on the screen — not handwritten.

**Page 1**

File Number: GZ-1994-3003
Event Name: Guizhou Dulaoying Region "12·1" Unexplained Destruction Incident Investigation Report (First Draft)

**Page 2**

Incident Overview: On December 1, 1994, at approximately 02:00, a loud explosion was heard in the mountainous area of Dulaoying, Guizhou Province. Local residents reported seeing a bright object streaking across the sky, followed by a violent tremor. The next morning, forestry workers discovered a large area of destroyed forest on a mountainside. The damaged area was roughly circular, approximately 200 meters in diameter. All trees within the area were snapped at the base or uprooted, with the tops pointing outward from the center. At the center of the damaged area, a circular pit approximately 5 meters in diameter and 3 meters deep was found. The pit walls were smooth and glassy, as if the rock had been melted by extreme heat.

Gu Yuan paused the video. He looked at the date: December 1, 1994. He remembered where he had been on that day — a third-year middle school student, preparing for his high school entrance exams. He had no memory of any such event.

He pressed play.

The video continued with interviews. A forestry worker, his face weathered, his hands rough, sat in front of a simple desk, speaking in thick Guizhou dialect. A young man in a military uniform sat across from him, asking questions.

"Where were you when you heard the sound?"

"I was in the forest ranger station, about two kilometers from the site."

"What did the sound sound like?"

"Very loud. Like thunder, but sharper. The ground shook."

"What did you see when you arrived at the site?"

"The trees were all knocked down. Like someone had pressed them flat with a giant hand. The center was a pit. The pit walls were like glass. Shiny."

"Had you ever seen anything like this before?"

"Never. Not in my whole life."

The interview continued. Other witnesses described similar accounts. A few reported seeing strange lights in the sky that night. One elderly woman claimed she saw a "flying fireball" fall from the sky and crash into the mountain.

Gu Yuan watched until the end. The video concluded with a summary: the cause of the incident remained undetermined. Possibilities included a meteorite impact, a lightning strike, or an explosion of unknown origin. But no meteorite fragments had been found. And the glassy pit walls suggested extreme heat — far beyond what a lightning strike could produce.

He stopped the video and sat in the darkness of the video room, thinking.

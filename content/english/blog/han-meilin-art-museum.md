On Sunday I took my little one to the Liyuan Dog Market to see the puppies. She was overjoyed and did not mind the swirling dog hair or the cacophony of barking at all. We browsed a few shops near the entrance but did not go deeper in — it was too chaotic, and the dogs there were enormous: Caucasian Shepherds, Tibetan Mastiffs, their barking frightening and their smell intimidating. After all, this was not the vast Qinghai-Tibet Plateau or the endless Caucasus Mountains.

After the dogs, I noticed my gas was low. There happened to be a Shell station near the Linheli subway stop, my preferred place to fill up — good quality, fair price, and though it was a bit far from home, it felt better than buying from those shameless state-owned enterprises that complain about losses all year round.

The Han Meilin Art Museum is right next to the Linheli gas station. I knew Han Meilin's work from my middle school days, when I saw his illustrations in youth literature magazines — furry little dogs, chubby little pigs, always天真调皮 and adorable. I even copied a couple. Later I heard news about him from time to time: he designed the Air China logo, and most recently, the Beijing Olympic Fuwa mascots.

In 2006, I passed by his earlier museum, tucked away on a narrow, bustling street in Yunjingli. Huge cast-metal components lay scattered on the ground, and on the支架 rested a nearly-finished work. It felt less like an art museum and more like a small hardware workshop — cluttered, worn, crowded. Yet the standing artworks revealed the grandeur of the artist's vision and the fineness of his craft. He was clearly no ordinary man.

The newly built museum has a distinctive character. The plain gray cement walls are made时尚 by wood grain patterns and nail holes. Six connected halls form the main structure. No windows, no doors — yet it does not feel oppressive or沉闷. The museum has three levels: one underground, two above (from what I could see). You enter on the ground floor via a long bridge. Inside, it is very quiet. The lighting gives only a limited brightness to the exhibits. The vast exhibition halls are serene and deep. A giant Buddha statue rises from the basement through the second floor, its brow reminiscent of the Yungang Great Buddha. The lines of the nose and eyes are smooth, light and shadow clearly defined — gentle yet firm, calm yet composed. The ancients said the work reflects the man. I imagine Han Meilin is much the same.

There were many exhibits, too many to list. The museum is full of touches: at the exit, a栈桥 made of small wooden blocks set in metal frames. Walking across it produces a clatter — each person leaves their own music, hurried or leisurely, composed or indifferent. Everyone walks, but not everyone hears.

Beneath the bridge lies a small pond, shallow but bringing a sense of流动 to the museum. To the east of the bridge, a few clumps of bamboo grow against a gray concrete wall. No brilliant red walls or green tiles — just the quiet tranquility of simplicity.

Even the landscaping trees were chosen with care: elegant red maples. Imagine in crisp autumn, a touch of red against the gray, ancient stone wall — what a scene that would be.

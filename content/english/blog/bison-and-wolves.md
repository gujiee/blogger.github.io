Do not pity the wolf, even though he is starving. Do not harbor the wolf, even though he is gentle as a dog. Do not cry for the wolf, even though he is beaten by the bison. If you help the wolf, the bison will die. And when there are no more bison, the starving wolf will turn on you.

It was the end of the 1990s. My friend from the Northwest told me this story:

On the vast, boundless grasslands, the herdsmen raise cattle, and the wolves hunt the cattle. For generations, this has been the cycle of the grasslands. The herdsmen love their cattle and hate the wolves. But lately, they have found it increasingly easy to raise cattle. Fewer and fewer calves are taken. More and more cattle reach maturity. The herdsmen's lives are improving. The wolf seems to have disappeared.

It has been replaced by a newly arrived wild bison. No one knows where it came from. It is huge, many times the size of an ox. When it appears on the grasslands, even the largest bulls dare not step forward. Occasionally, a brave bull charges at it, but the bison lowers its head, and with a thrust of its sharp horns, disembowels the bull without ceremony. The herdsmen's dogs bark furiously but dare not close in. The herdsmen are afraid of this enormous creature and have no way to deal with it. Were it not for the wolf, the bison would have no natural enemy on the grasslands.

But now there are almost no wolves. In the past, with wolves around, although the calves were often taken, the bison never dared to come near. Once the wolves were eliminated, the bison arrived. The leaders were confused. They could not understand this. They said: how can wolves protect the cattle? Wolves eat the cattle — how could they protect them? Nature is a mysterious thing. A chain connects everything. Break a link in the chain, and the balance is lost.

So the herdsmen began to welcome the wolves back. They stopped hunting them. They protected the wolf's habitat. And soon the wolves returned. The wolves drove the bison away. A few calves were taken, but far fewer than before. The wolves and the bison had both reminded the herdsmen of one thing: not to break the chain.

In the early morning, the sky had not yet fully brightened. The world was immersed in a peaceful, slightly cool mist. In the east, where the sky met the distant horizon, there appeared an extremely subtle, pale purple dawn glow. It was not the bright blue of midday, nor the passionate red of dusk. It carried a dreamlike stillness and tenderness unique to the hour before dawn — as if the last sigh of the departing night.

The tall cabbage tree in the yard was still wrapped in朦胧 morning light, like a silent giant. Suddenly, a burst of noisy birdsong, like the opening of a meeting, shattered the stillness. The sound was clear and urgent, as if telling the whole yard: a new day has begun.

A tui, its plumage a flash of violet-blue, burst like lightning from the dense, dark green leaves of the cabbage tree. Its feathers shimmered like velvet in the first light, and the two bright white tufts on its throat, like a priest's collar, stood out vividly against the dark background. Like an elegant messenger bearing some urgent mission, it cut through the morning. Then several smaller birds, as if receiving orders, followed it out from the foliage. They formed a loose formation and flew straight toward that pale purple dawn light, soon disappearing into the shifting clouds of the eastern sky.

A plump kereru had landed, unnoticed, on the worn wooden fence. It paced leisurely, its heavy body swaying slightly with each step, looking somewhat clumsy yet carrying an innocent, carefree air. It lowered its head from time to time to preen its feathers, then lifted it again to coo at the empty yard — a monotone, drawn-out sound. The cooing was low and round, like an ancient bell tolling from the depth of the yard, making the morning feel even more peaceful and slow.

Two fantails, like a perfectly matched pair of dancers, flitted and chased each other among the dense branches of the feijoa tree in the corner of the yard. Their small bodies were agile, their long tail feathers sweeping graceful arcs through the air as if swirling skirts. They would hover in place, wings vibrating, chirping softly; then dive, landing lightly on another branch. Their dance was like a cheerful, carefree tango, every move filled with the joy and vitality of life.

In the northeast corner of the yard, the plum tree we had planted years ago had grown over three meters tall, its trunk sturdy, its crown spreading like a canopy. Days earlier, it had been covered in snow-white blossoms, blooming with abandon in the spring breeze. Now, the flowering season had passed. After a spring rain, the delicate petals had withered and fallen silently, forming a thin layer beneath the tree like remnants of unmelted snow. In their place, countless tender green leaves pushed out eagerly from the branches, filling the tree with a quiet, restrained life force.

Beside the plum tree, the pittosporum we had transplanted from a friend's house were at their most vibrant. Their new leaves were a strange color between pink and brown-red, striking and vivid against the surrounding green. They had a strong, distinctive fragrance, much like the Chinese toon leaves we used to eat in spring in our hometown. A few days ago, overwhelmed by homesickness, we picked a bunch and made a fragrant "toon" omelet. Though the taste was slightly different from the real thing, the familiar flavor from a distant homeland brought a touch of warmth to our days in this foreign land.

Now the sun had finally risen above the distant trees. Golden light filtered through the mist and fell slantingly into the yard. Light and shadow began to move, tracing clear, contrasting outlines on the grass, along the walls, on the veins of every leaf. Dewdrops on the grass sparkled like diamonds in the sunlight.

The air began to carry that particular warmth and freshness that comes when sunlight draws moisture from the earth — mixed with the fragrance of grass and the faint sweetness of unknown flowers.

I sat on the wooden chair on the deck, holding a cup of warm tea, quietly watching this vibrant tableau of sounds, colors, and light. I suddenly felt that this might be life at its most真实 and beautiful. No noise, no conflict. Every living thing followed the rhythm of nature, existing freely and fully in its own way. The calls of birds, the pacing of the pigeon, the growth of leaves, the fading of flowers — all of it formed a harmonious, perfect order.

And I, an accidental observer who had wandered into this scene, could only sit here quietly, feeling, listening, becoming part of this peaceful and beautiful morning. For just a moment — even if only for a moment — to forget all worldly troubles and cares, and let my heart, like the plants in this yard, breathe freely in the morning sun.

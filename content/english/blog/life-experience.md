I love listening to cross-talk. Hou Baolin, Liu Baorui, Ma Sanli — I enjoy them all. Ma Ji is good too. The younger generation, I rarely listen to. Sometimes I laugh, but afterward I cannot remember what was so funny. Maybe I am getting older; my memory is not what it used to be.

But Guo Degang's cross-talk brought back my old memory. This bald, big-headed man — at first I did not like him. He had no trace of culture about him, only a ruffian air. The old saying goes: "Never judge a book by its cover." It could not be more fitting than applied to him.

I have listened to a lot of Guo Degang's cross-talk, though I have never been to the teahouse at Tiantan. I download them from the internet onto my MP3 player. Like Guo before he made it, I am a poor man. I have always wished I could see him perform live, to shout and cheer with the crowd. But going into the city to hear a show — by the time it ends, the buses have stopped running, and I cannot afford a taxi. Walking from the theater to Tongzhou would take until dawn, and the next day I would still have to go to work and earn a living. The plight of a poor man — Master Guo surely knows it better than I do.

This is why I love his cross-talk. The thoughts and joys and sorrows of ordinary people are captured perfectly by his eloquent tongue. "I'm in the Mafia" is my favorite piece. When he talks about eating sunflower seeds at a seafood restaurant, or cycling from the north end of the city to the south, I cannot help but laugh — doubled over, tears streaming. But when the laughter fades, a faint sadness creeps in, and my eyes grow moist. A friend of mine, a tea connoisseur named Chang, once told me: good tea is always bitter first, and the sweetness that lingers afterward is long and deep. Guo Degang's cross-talk is the opposite — sweet first, then bitter; laugh first, then grieve. And the bitterness, set against the sweetness, has a flavor all its own.

A man cannot produce such brilliant cross-talk without having lived through hard times. Master Guo's years of poverty and struggle in Beijing surely have something to do with it. Many things come from experience.

Mencius said: "When Heaven is about to place a great responsibility on a man, it first vexes his mind, wearies his body, starves his flesh, leaves him destitute, and frustrates his endeavors — thereby arousing his spirit and strengthening his character."

I believe this wholeheartedly. It is the truth. For how can a man know the happiness of wealth without having known poverty? How can he know the value of a full meal without having known hunger? How can he know the hardship of labor without having worked? How can he know the simple joy of an ordinary life without having suffered?

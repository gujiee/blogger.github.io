Singapore — a tiny nation straddling the equator. Its total land area is just over 700 square kilometers, less than one ten-thousandth of China. Even compared to its neighbors Malaysia and Indonesia, it's laughably small. Yet this "speck of land" commands immense global respect in economics, politics, and human development — even the formidable United States cannot afford to ignore it. That alone was reason enough for me to come and see for myself.

I flew in from Jakarta, Indonesia. It was not an easy trip — my mind was cluttered with extraneous worries unrelated to work. When the first equatorial rays pierced the clouds and lit up the cabin, when the AirAsia jet skimmed over the sapphire sea, when the countless ships scattered like stars across the water came into view, followed by a lush green land rushing toward me — I knew Singapore had arrived.

The first surprise was how smooth the immigration process was. No endless queues like at Chinese customs. No officials with that habitual cold, haughty gaze. It felt like walking into a well-managed museum — hand over your documents and passport, the officer stamps and returns them in seconds. No suspicion, no interrogation. It gave me a long-lost sense of being welcomed home.

How sad, really, that I rarely feel that踏实 sense of belonging when I return to my own country.

---

At Changi Airport, I exchanged a thousand Singapore dollars, bought a local SIM card, and walked toward the taxi stand. Another surprise: almost no one was waiting in line. Instead, a few gleaming taxis sat quietly in the nearly empty parking lot, waiting for passengers.

The hotel my company booked was in Geylang. I'd checked before my trip — Geylang is near Singapore's famous red-light district. Word had it that after dark, you could see seductive women in various national costumes strolling by the streets or in windows. The hotel itself was decent. Compared to the claustrophobic "cage buildings" of Hong Kong, this was luxurious. A clean, tidy bed, a spacious bathroom, and outside the window, a lush, garden-like view. The front desk staff, with their sincere smiles like younger siblings, made me feel exceptionally comfortable and at ease.

---

About two stops north of the hotel on foot was the MRT station. The road was lined with restaurants, most of them Chinese: Dongbei dumplings, Hunan cured meats, Sichuan, Guangdong, Fujian flavors... interspersed with Indian, Vietnamese, and Malay restaurants — a true fusion of Southeast Asian character.

Unlike the sprawling, dust-choked construction sites that blanket China, Singapore has vast expanses of immaculate green spaces. No random buildings on the lawns, no trampled dirt paths. Enormous tropical plants grow naturally across even, well-kept grass. These green spaces lie quietly, luxuriously, right in the heart of the city. Back in China, on land worth its weight in gold, such a thing would be an impossible miracle.

---

There weren't many cars on Singapore's roads — nothing like the endless, gridlocked traffic jams of China. I was told the government strictly limits private car ownership through high costs, making it prohibitively expensive to own a car here. On the other hand, the public transport system is remarkably developed. The MRT is the city's arterial system, while buses serve as capillaries, precisely channeling people to every district.

Most roads here are narrow. Crossing the street doesn't feel like venturing onto a battlefield. Pedestrians and drivers alike follow the rules scrupulously. Some intersections don't even have traffic lights. The first time I approached one of these, I was nervous — I stood at the curb, looking left and right, dreading that at any moment an F1-qualifying driver would come barreling down the road, just like back home.

Then something astonishing happened. A car approached and began to slow down a full ten meters before the intersection. I watched the driver warily, unsure of his intentions. I wanted to cross, but I was afraid he might suddenly accelerate, forcing me to scramble back. Then the driver, through the windshield, smiled and gestured for me to go. I still doubted — was he really yielding to me? I looked behind me. No one else. Only then did I believe it.

For the first time in my life, I truly understood what it felt like to cross a street without fear, with dignity intact.

---

I studied the MRT map carefully. My original plan was to ride straight to the city center. But when I arrived and saw I still had time, I changed my mind — I rode two more stops, then decided to walk back slowly. I ended up near Chinatown. I got off, bought some drinks, and started wandering back along the elevated rail tracks.

The streetscape wasn't particularly stunning architecturally, but the dominant color was always green. Expansive lawns, towering trees — many buildings were quietly hidden behind foliage. The streets themselves weren't new — I could see patches of asphalt repair everywhere — but they were remarkably clean. No scraps of paper on the ground, no plastic bags, no unsightly spittle. Occasionally someone would jog past me from behind, their steps light and easy even when crossing intersections. A few cars in the distance waited silently for pedestrians — no irritable honking, no impatient screeching of brakes. Everything felt so peaceful, so natural.

---

In that rare tranquility, I gradually relaxed — I even forgot I was in a foreign country. I just kept walking, until I suddenly realized I was lost.

I had a free map from the hotel, but in a completely unfamiliar neighborhood, even the map was useless. At first I wasn't worried. I headed toward a nearby overpass, trusting my instincts. When I got there, I realized I was going the wrong way. I turned back and made a right into a residential area. Most of the faces were Chinese. Hidden inside was a traditional Chinese wet market.

At a small shop, I bought a bottle of water and asked the shopkeeper for directions to the MRT station. He didn't understand Chinese. I switched to English. Still nothing. Just then, a young couple walked by. The shopkeeper called them over to help. I tried Chinese first — no luck. Then English — this time they responded. They kindly pointed the way — which still pointed toward that same overpass.

When I found myself back at the overpass, dejected, I finally felt a flicker of fatigue and frustration. I sat down on a clean step and watched the traffic flow by. Now I was truly stumped.

Then I saw a young girl approaching from a distance. When she got close, I greeted her politely in English and asked for directions. After a couple of sentences, she waved her hands — she clearly hadn't understood. I quickly opened my map and pointed, explaining slowly in English. She was still confused. But when her eyes fell on the place names I'd annotated in Chinese, they lit up. In flawless Mandarin, she said: "Can you speak Chinese?"

In that moment, on that equatorial foreign street, hearing my mother tongue spoken with such clarity — the relief was indescribable. Like grabbing a lifeline.

Learning that I was new to Singapore, she was incredibly warm. "Come on, I'll take you to the station." On the way, we chatted a bit. She was Chinese, had come to work in Singapore not long ago. Her monthly salary was about 2,500 Singapore dollars (roughly 12,500 RMB) — the lowest bracket here. She said she really wanted to go back to China, but couldn't find a decent job there — the pay would only be about 1,100 RMB, barely enough to survive. So she had no choice but to leave home and come work thousands of miles away.

---

When I reached the city center, I found a sculpture marking Singapore's birthplace, surrounded by towering skyscrapers. This is Singapore's financial heart — the heart of all Asia. Almost every top international bank has a presence here.

Behind the Maybank building, facing the Singapore River, stood the Bank of China. In front of the building were several bronze statues full of period character: one group showed bullock cart drivers — bare-backed, dark-skinned, their long braids coiled on their heads — one loading goods onto the cart, another reaching out as if asking for wages. Another group showed a Westerner lecturing two Orientals; one of them wore a little skullcap and embroidered silk jacket over cloth shoes. Just from their dress, these statues silently told the story of the first Chinese laborers and merchants who came south to Nanyang, living humbly and struggling to get by on this distant land.

---

A steel bridge spans the Singapore River, all welded fixed-arch construction. It reminded me a bit of Lanzhou's Zhongshan Bridge, but with two massive arched steel beams that made it look more modern. The entire bridge was painted white — against the green trees on both banks and the blue sky, it looked exceptionally clean and crisp.

The bridge was built in 1868. That same year — the seventh year of Tongzhi in China's Qing Dynasty — the Qing government was mired in internal and external crises, struggling to suppress a wave of popular uprisings: bloodily crushing the Nian Army in the north and the Hui Rebellion in the northwest. Yet on the other side of the world, in that very same year, the American inventor Edison was developing the incandescent lamp that would soon illuminate countless homes. And in the island nation of Japan to China's east, the Meiji Restoration — which would completely transform the country's destiny — was just beginning its dramatic overture.

---

Tucked away in an inconspicuous corner at the bridgehead were two more delightful little sculptures: on one end, a large cat crouched lazily beside the bridge pier, watching two mischievous kittens play-fighting at its feet; on the other, several bare-chested urchins were frozen mid-leap, diving from the bridge railing into the river.

These vintage figures, frozen in the early nineteenth century, mingled with modern office workers in suits, talking on phones, hurrying across the bridge — creating a powerful, surreal sense of time colliding. For just a moment, I felt as though I had truly traveled through time.

---

Crossing the bridge, though only a river away from the financial district, revealed a completely different kind of stillness. Dense groves of trees, wide lawns, quiet walking paths. A pristine white memorial church (St. Andrew's Cathedral) stood in the distance, nestled in deep shade.

Passing through a pedestrian tunnel to the other side, I took in the most iconic Singapore landmarks: the Singapore Flyer, the two buildings shaped like enormous durians (the Esplanade), and next to the Flyer, the Marina Bay Sands hotel — three towers supporting what looked like a giant ship beached on their rooftops.

Everywhere I went, I saw no discarded trash or scraps of paper. Even when a dead leaf just fell or a broken branch was blown down by the wind, uniformed cleaners would appear like silent ghosts, swiftly sweeping everything away.

---

Speaking of Singapore's near-obsessive "cleanliness and order," I have to mention its laws.

Singapore's legal system is exceptionally detailed and strictly enforced — many who don't understand it even call it "torture." Things that are taken for granted in China, even considered trivial — spitting on the ground, dropping a piece of paper, or taking a sip of water on the subway — carry hefty fines in Singapore that make your wallet ache. For serious crimes that harm society, the punishments (like caning) are beyond what most Chinese can imagine.

Yet when I looked closely at the faces of Singaporeans who live under these supposedly "draconian laws," I saw not a trace of fear, oppression, or anxious caution.

As a friend who has lived here for years told me: "In Singapore, you just need to follow the rules. As long as you play by them, even if you're a beggar, the government won't randomly arrest you, harass you, or punish you."

More fundamentally, Singapore's judiciary demonstrates an exceptionally strong commitment to fairness. Whether you're an American citizen from a superpower or a Southeast Asian laborer from a neighboring poor country, if you break the same law, you face exactly the same punishment. Even when then-US President Bill Clinton personally intervened on behalf of the American teenager Michael Fay, who had been sentenced to caning for vandalism, the Singapore government did not yield an inch.

---

Han Feizi, the ancient Chinese legalist philosopher, asserted over two thousand years ago: *"When the law fails, it is because those at the top break it."* In other words, the collapse of law begins with those in power refusing to obey it. He also lamented: *"The trouble with law is not that it is too harsh, but that it is unjust."*

When justice — that absolute prerequisite — is stripped from the rule of law, "harsh laws" become a meat grinder for the powerful to oppress the weak. Society becomes filled with fear and aggression. But when the legal system achieves absolute fairness, justice, and transparency, even the harshest punishments become armor that protects every law-abiding citizen's freedom to breathe.

Looking at Singapore's orderly, green, thriving landscape, I was finally convinced: a nation's true strength and civilization are never measured by the density of its skyscrapers, but by whether its scales of justice remain perfectly level for everyone.

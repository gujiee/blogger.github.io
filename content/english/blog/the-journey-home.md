I tossed and turned through the night, unable to find rest. By the early hours, the window was still wrapped in thick darkness, but I could no longer keep my eyes shut. Beside me, my wife slept soundly — I listened to her steady, shallow breathing and couldn't bear to wake her from her难得的 good sleep. So I lay there, eyes open, body rigid, letting my thoughts wander in circles, unwilling to move. At least it was Sunday — no work to rush to. That hazy, half-dreaming night slowly wore itself out in silent endurance.

As the sky began to lighten, my phone screen suddenly lit up. A WeChat message from my mother — just a few short lines, every word piercing: "Come back soon." I stared at the screen for a long time, then forced myself to sit up. I opened the airline app, cancelled my original May 7th ticket, and without hesitation rebooked for that very day — May 3rd, China Southern flight CZ306, with a transfer at Guangzhou Baiyun Airport.

The moment I pressed the payment button, something inside me went hollow. I got up silently and started packing clothes for the trip home. I gathered my work phone and gas card, returned them to the office, and texted my supervisor. By the time I finished, it was nearly 11 a.m. My wife Weiwei could see the anguish on my face and urged me to lie down and rest. But how could I sleep? My heart felt overgrown with weeds, blocked by stones. I wandered aimlessly around the yard, found a tool, and secured the wobbly solar light firmly to the wall. I checked the yard drain — blocked again, as usual — so I rolled up my sleeves and cleaned it out, bit by bit, enduring the filth. As if keeping my hands busy could briefly shield me from the reality I was about to face.

When there were no more chores left, I sat down on the deck, defeated. The deep-autumn wind of the Southern Hemisphere carried a chill. I looked up at the clouds drifting overhead — gathering and dispersing endlessly — and felt a deep, crushing helplessness rising from the bottom of my heart.

At 5 p.m., we set off for the airport. It was a stark, lonely autumn evening. The sunset spread across the sky like molten gold, but layers of clouds pressed down heavy as lead. My daughter drove, carrying us slowly across the Harbour Bridge with its forest of masts. Along the way, my wife and daughter spoke very little.

Watching the scenery recede outside the window, my heart ached with reluctance. I couldn't bear to leave them. I couldn't bear to leave this warm, lived-in home: the trees in the yard half-pruned, the drain cleared but probably still clogged somewhere, the mold stain from dampness spreading on the southwest corner of my daughter's ceiling that I hadn't yet dealt with. Tomorrow Weiwei would have a tooth pulled — she'd be in pain. And my daughter had important exams coming up. With me gone, who would take care of everything? Even tonight, I couldn't tie on my apron and cook them a warm dinner anymore.

But I had to go back to Beijing. Even if it meant splitting my heart in two, I had to go. This journey halfway across the world was to see my father, who didn't have much time left.

I remember visiting him last year. His mind was already like an erased blackboard — he had completely forgotten his most beloved son. He held my hand tightly, his eyes cloudy and confused, and asked, "Who are you?" In that moment, my heart shattered into pieces. But at least then he was still healthy — he could walk with a cane, he could still eat. How, in just one short year, had the mountain of a man I called Father become bedridden? My mother said his fever wouldn't break, that he could barely eat or drink, that his once-broad back was now withered like a dry branch.

Ah... whenever I think of my father lying sick in bed, it becomes hard to breathe. My heart feels crushed under an immense weight, sinking into an abyss.

Why must life hold so many partings, so much sorrow and pain? Could it not be a little kinder to us, granting even a single moment more of joy? Right now, I wish I could find a quiet corner and drink an entire bottle of amber liquor alone. One sip — let the burning liquid sear through my throat and make me forget the tangled burdens of this world. Another sip — let me forget who I am. One more big gulp — and perhaps, in this vast world, I could find half a day without worry.

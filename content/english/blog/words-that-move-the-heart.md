As a少年, I did not love words. I did not love literature. I loved angles, acceleration, reaction formulas. Even in Chinese class, I often studied the "knowledge" I loved then. I believed that this "knowledge" was power, was ability. Master it, and you could build enormous machines, travel to outer space, dive to the bottom of the sea — there was nothing you could not do. Words, to me, were meaningless.

I knew only Einstein, Newton, Faraday. Sanmao and Qiong Yao were distant and hollow to me. The great poets — Li Bai, Du Fu, Su Dongpo, Xu Zhimo — were mere symbols to be memorized for a good grade.

At thirty, I suddenly realized I had been wrong. Devastatingly wrong. By then, that "knowledge" was only scraps and leftovers. Even calculating a slope required a math handbook. In truth, all I had retained and could use from those years were the four basic arithmetic operations.

But I no longer cared about that "knowledge." We built the Great Wall, yet it did not keep out northern invaders. Today, aside from a few relatively intact sections, the rest is just earthen mounds. The Hanging Gardens of Babylon vanished in the fires of war long ago. The Titanic, proclaimed unsinkable, sank on its maiden voyage into the cold, dark sea, taking countless lives with it. The space program was paved with human lives — Russian cosmonauts burned alive in their spacecraft, the Columbia exploded before the world's eyes, seven astronauts vanishing without a trace.

Who remembers who built the Great Wall? Who designed the Hanging Gardens? Who constructed the Titanic? Who vanished with the Columbia? Without dusting off ancient historical records, I believe very few people know.

Yet lines like "Wild grasses spread across the plain / each year they wither and thrive" or "Moonlight before my bed / could it be frost on the ground?" — these are recited even by Chinese children who have never attended school. And these poems are over a thousand years old. Compared to the century-old Titanic or the decades-old Columbia, the power of these simple words is astonishing.

Shakespeare wrote with such conviction:

> Not marble, not the gilded monuments
> Of princes, shall outlive this powerful rhyme
> But you shall shine more bright in these contents
> Than unswept stone, besmeared with sluttish time.

I discovered that I had fallen in love with words. These simple yet infinitely complex words. These words that move the heart.

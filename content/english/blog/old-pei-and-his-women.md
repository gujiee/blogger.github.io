Xining in June or July is at its most un-Northwestern.

It sheds the dry, biting face of winter and spring — when dust storms roll across the sky at the slightest wind — and becomes exceptionally gentle. The sky is a shade of azure so pure it feels almost extravagant, with huge white clouds hung low overhead like cotton candy casually kneaded by unseen hands — so close you feel you could reach up and touch their softness. The air is crisp enough to clean your lungs. The sun is strong, stinging against the skin, but the moment you step into the shade, a breeze carrying the scent of grass and the chill of snow-capped mountains wraps around you, carrying away the summer heat.

---

The project kept me busy for over a month. To be close to the work, I rented an apartment in a place called Jiaotong Lane, between Shengli Road and Wusi Road. It was an old residential compound belonging to the Qinghai Agriculture and Animal Husbandry Group — red brick walls covered in the mottled traces of time, the stairwell perpetually filled with the aroma of braised lamb and pulled noodles. The apartment was a standard three-bedroom. The walls still bore the stubborn crayon scribbles of the previous tenant's child — crooked little figures. It was dated, but quiet and spacious.

Every evening after work, walking through the lane lined with towering white poplars, watching the neighbors battle it out over chess in the courtyard, hearing the children race past on their bicycles with shouts and laughter — in those moments, my outsider's heart would slowly settle, wrapped in the rich, lived-in warmth of it all.

My client was Qinghai Sidun Group, headquartered in the imposing Qinghai Agriculture and Animal Husbandry Building on the south side of People's Park, just steps from where I lived. But the Sidun Potato Company factory was farther out, in the biological development zone along the Huangshui River — the old meat processing plant.

The company's manager was surnamed Pei. I called him Manager Pei. Privately, I called him Old Pei.

---

I first met Old Pei at a bidding meeting the year before. Though he was the manager of Weston, from the very start of the meeting he showed clear favoritism. During the technical defense session, his刁钻刻薄 questions came at us like Northwestern arrows — sharp and cold, straight for the heart. Back then, I was wary and hostile toward him. I thought he was just a slick, shrewd businessman who would stop at nothing to get what he wanted.

But fate has a way of playing its bland jokes. We ended up winning the project, and my former adversary became my client. Old Pei — a man standing over 1.8 meters tall, a crane among chickens in this crowd of Northwestern men — became the person I dealt with every day.

For a Northwesterner, he was remarkably pale. With his not-quite-standard round face and the slightly greasy, shoulder-length center-part hairstyle popular in that era, he gave off a fragile, intellectual air. Perhaps because he was so tall, he tended to hunch slightly when he walked — a posture that diffused the intimidation of his height and instead conveyed a kind of excessive humility. His perennial uniform was a well-fitting but slightly worn dark blue or gray casual suit, with a black leather handbag worn at the left armpit, its edges frayed. When he opened his mouth, out came a soft yet firm Qinghai-accented Mandarin, delivered in a characteristic drawn-out cadence.

As equipment installation and debugging progressed, the formal distance between us gradually dissolved over countless meals and late-night work sessions. Old Pei was talkative, especially after a few glasses of liquor — his big eyes would light up.

He used to be a researcher at the Qinghai Provincial Animal Husbandry Research Institute — a bona fide intellectual. Later, Sidun Group poached him as a senior management talent. The company car assigned to him was a half-worn Great Wall pickup truck, still bearing the company logo in large letters, its original color long since indiscernible. This truck — which rattled at every joint — became our primary mode of transportation as we穿梭 through the streets of Xining. And it was during these rides that I began to catch glimpses of Old Pei's hidden, complicated emotional world.

---

The first to catch my attention was the company accountant, Xiao Zhang. She was a recent graduate from Shaanxi — not particularly striking in the face, but with an exceptionally full figure. Her clothes were nothing flashy, but no matter what she wore, it always seemed barely able to contain her. Not long after the project began, Old Pei started bringing Xiao Zhang to my dinner parties regularly. She was quiet, rarely even lifting her head to look at others. She just sat beside Old Pei, carefully剔去 fish bones for him, refilling his glass, her eyes turning into a pool of spring water whenever they landed on him — full of dependence and adoration.

The gossip about them had already reached my ears. I'm a traditional person, and I had a instinctive aversion to this kind of ambiguous extramarital affair — "old ox eating tender grass." But at the dinner table, as the contractor waiting for the client to sign off on the project, all I could do was maintain a polite smile.

---

One time, when a major project milestone was completed successfully, Old Pei arranged a celebration dinner at a Sichuan restaurant on Dashizi. Eleven people drank twenty-four bottles of highland barley liquor.

After the meal, Old Pei's white face flushed red. He leaned close to my ear and whispered mysteriously: "Engineer Gu, come on, let me take you somewhere 'nice' — a real place to relax."

Before I could decline, he pulled me into that battered pickup truck without ceremony. Xiao Zhang silently took the back seat.

The truck drove north, to an old district called Xiaoqiao. The streets were narrow, lined with cramped, brightly lit little shops — messy and worn-down. The car stopped at a lane entrance. Across the street, a "Jin Qiao Hotel" sign with missing letters flickered half-heartedly in the darkness.

Old Pei led us, with the familiarity of a regular, into a tiny barbershop with no sign at all. The place was minuscule, filled with the cloying sweetness of cheap shampoo. The moment we walked in, Old Pei acted like he was home — he didn't even need to exchange pleasantries. He directed me to lie down on the shop's only reclining barber chair, then called out toward the back room. A middle-aged woman in her forties emerged.

"Give Engineer Gu a shoulder rub — he's been exhausted lately," Old Pei instructed, then sank comfortably into the worn-out fabric sofa.

What surprised me was Xiao Zhang's reaction. She walked in, put down her bag as naturally as can be, picked up a broom, and began sweeping the hair clippings off the floor. Then she went to the back, wrung out a hot towel, and even熟练ly made change for a customer who had just walked in. Between her and the middle-aged woman — whom Old Pei called Sister Liu — there was an默契 that needed no words.

I lay on the chair, letting Sister Liu's rough but powerful hands work through my shoulders and neck — pain and comfort mingling. But inside, I couldn't help wondering: Sister Liu, at her age — could she be Xiao Zhang's relative? Or even... her mother?

The mystery wasn't solved until a later drinking session when Old Pei let the truth slip. That night, he was really drunk, his eyes glazed. He told me that Sister Liu wasn't Xiao Zhang's mother at all — she was his "old flame" from his research institute days. Later, when the institute restructured, Sister Liu was laid off and had no way to support herself. Old Pei didn't hesitate — he dug into his private savings and bought this little shop for her.

---

Time flowed like the Huangshui River, rushing day and night. Seven years passed in a blink.

Seven years later, in Shanghai. The late autumn wind had turned cold. At a dazzling international food machinery exhibition, I ran into Old Pei again by chance.

He was still tall, but hunched more noticeably. His signature center-part hairstyle had thinned considerably, revealing large patches of pale scalp. The wrinkles at the corners of his eyes were like the gullies of the Loess Plateau — deep and clear.

We caught up over a meal at a Shanghai-style restaurant by the Huangpu River. A bottle of warmed yellow wine, a few delicate dishes so fine they barely filled the gaps between teeth. Old Pei's tolerance for alcohol had clearly declined. After a few cups, his eyes began to glaze over.

That night, he got completely drunk. I had to half-carry, half-drag this tall, drunken man back to my hotel room. He collapsed onto the soft bed like a pile of mud, mumbling incoherently, his fingers still stubbornly swiping across his phone screen, dialing one call after another. I couldn't make out who he was calling — only that his voice carried an indescribable weariness, complaint, and a pitiful, almost begging tenderness.

Just as I finished showering and was about to turn in, the doorbell rang.

I opened the door. A young woman stood in the hallway. She looked twenty-seven or twenty-eight, wearing a well-tailored short goose-yellow woolen coat over a half-length, elegant work skirt, revealing a section of pale calf. Her face was delicate, her skin fair.

Startled by the unexpected visitor, I stood frozen. The woman, however, was unflustered. She gave me a polite smile and asked softly, in standard Mandarin: "Excuse me, is Manager Pei here?" If you listened closely, you could detect a faint Northwestern accent beneath the Mandarin.

She entered the room, placed an elegant paper bag she was carrying on the bedside table, then sat down naturally beside Old Pei's bed. From the bag, she took out a few golden oranges. She removed her coat and draped it over the back of a chair. She picked up an orange and, with slender fingers, unhurriedly peeled it, carefully placing segments into the half-reclining Old Pei's mouth. As he chewed, still drunk, he mumbled curses. The woman was neither hurried nor annoyed — she simply watched him tenderly, letting his wandering hand rest on her waist.

In that moment, I felt like an extra, untimely guest. Silently, I picked up my room card and wallet, stepped out of the room, went to the front desk, and booked a new room for myself.

Later, when Old Pei sobered up, he told me, somewhat sheepishly, that the woman's surname was Li. She was one of his paramours — a young woman who had come from the Northwest to Shanghai for graduate studies.

I listened in silence. I thought of the dimly lit hair salon in Xiaoqiao, Xining, seven years ago. I thought of the middle-aged woman named Liu. And I thought of the quiet young woman from Shaanxi, Xiao Zhang, who had just graduated and used to sit beside Old Pei, picking up food for him.

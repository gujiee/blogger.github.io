At noon, bright sunlight warmed the room like early spring. My Milan plant basked lazily in the sun, its glossy leaves scattering the warm light through every corner of the living room.

I remember it was spring 2002 — a beautiful spring. We moved from Liuliqiao to Shiliuyuan in Liujiayao. It was a small one-bedroom apartment, facing east and west, about forty square meters, but it was enough for us. After years of drifting, we had nothing. The only furniture we owned was a double bed we bought for a little over 400 yuan at the Guangcai Market and a simple bookshelf for 80 yuan. When we laid down the mattress and put our books on the shelf, that dim little room already felt like home.

That spring, we bought some plants to liven up the place: a larger Milan and a smaller asparagus fern. I have always loved Milan — its glossy, full leaves, its lush, thriving form, and its tiny, unremarkable yellow flowers that fill the air with fragrance. The scent of Milan always reminds me of a line of poetry: "Like distant, ethereal music" — mysterious and far-reaching.

From the day I brought them home, I cared for them. Whenever I watered them or trimmed their leaves, my worries seemed to fade. And whenever I saw new buds or flowers forming, I was filled with indescribable excitement. I would check on those tiny buds every day, eager to see how they were growing.

In the two years at Liujiayao, my Milan produced buds only once, but they withered before they could bloom. At the end of 2003, we moved from Liujiayao to Building 6 of the Agricultural Machinery Institute in Beishatan. The new place had two bedrooms, and though it was old, it had a balcony facing south. I thought the warm sunlight would help the plants thrive. But things went very wrong. Perhaps the plant was hurt by the cold during the move, or perhaps it could not adjust to the new home. The glossy leaves began to dry up and fall off, one by one. At first, I tried everything — moving the Milan out of direct sunlight, adding fertilizer — hoping to stop the leaf loss. But the opposite happened. The leaves fell faster. Before long, that graceful, lush Milan was gone, replaced by bare, naked branches. It was heartbreaking.

I consulted books and searched online for ways to save my Milan. I tried one method after another. A month or two passed, and the poor plant remained the same. I could not bear to throw it away. I kept watering it regularly, loosening the soil, and even caught a few playful earthworms from the grass downstairs after the rain, putting them in the Milan's pot.

As the days grew hotter, the Milan showed no improvement. But the earthworms had turned the soil dark and rich. I watered it more frequently. Though the Milan was still bare branches, in my heart I already knew the answer. I continued to care for it, without a moment's slack.

One morning, four or five months later, I noticed tiny green dots on the bare branches. They were so small that without careful attention, you would miss them. These green dots appeared all over the branches. Only with a magnifying glass could you see they were tiny new buds.

The Milan, like a girl who had slept long, eagerly made up for lost time. The energy stored during those months unfolded like magic in the summer. Every day — every hour, every minute — it was changing. In less than a week, a lush, vibrant Milan appeared again on my balcony. In the days that followed, it bloomed, one batch after another, from summer through winter. That year, the house was filled with the gentle fragrance of Milan. And with that持久 fragrance, our lives, too, grew better and better.

Food and morality — like a donkey's lips and a horse's mouth, they do not belong together. Whoever had the imagination to connect such distant things deserves a place in the Guinness Book of World Records for "Most Creative Mind."

But our food supply really does have problems. Forget the "water-injected pork" and "sick chicken meat" — just the 2008 melamine milk scandal, this year's "dyed steamed buns," and the "lean meat powder" are enough to make anyone's heart pound and their appetite vanish. Fortunately, humans are special animals. We think. We solve problems. And the Chinese, in particular, have a unique approach: when a problem arises, first try to make a big problem small and a small problem disappear. When you can no longer hide it, and it becomes too difficult to solve, you cut the Gordian knot — swift and clean. But these problems are like spring grass on the wasteland: cut them down, and they grow right back.

We dealt with melamine milk. But who can guarantee that no one will add other chemicals to milk? And who can guarantee that the feed given to cows is safe and chemical-free? We dealt with lean meat powder. But who can guarantee there is no "fresh meat powder" or "tenderizer powder"? We have hundreds of millions of chemical compounds in the world. Who can guarantee that every single one can be detected by modern equipment?

After countless rounds of reflection, food has finally been linked to morality. It seems the root of the food problem has been found. And once you find the root, a solution cannot be far behind.

But just as food and the most basic human morality have been linked in China, and just as the Chinese people have poured their righteous indignation onto food companies, I have unexpectedly noticed the following:

1. The milk powder that Sanlu exported to Southeast Asia, and even to Africa, did not have excess melamine.
2. The pigs that China exported to Hong Kong had not been fed lean meat powder.
3. The food that China exported to the United States does not seem to have caused any problems.

If we are to calculate by probability, I cannot help but marvel at how unlucky the Chinese people — with five thousand years of history — truly are. If we are to explain this by morality, I cannot help but question: does morality have a double standard — one for inside, and one for outside?

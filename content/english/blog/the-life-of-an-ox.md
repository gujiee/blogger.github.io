In the early morning light, sunlight poured over the distant mountaintop, spilling clear and bright onto every inch of the small village. The village had not yet fully awakened — a few wisps of cooking smoke rose from scattered chimneys. An old farmer, holding a long pipe in one hand and leading a sturdy yellow ox with the other, walked toward the distant mountains now disappearing into the morning mist. Several cows and calves followed behind, strolling leisurely, letting out contented lowing as they faced the dew-wet grass by the roadside. Their calls, mingling with the moist air and the scent of grass and burning firewood, relaxed my long-taut nerves. I breathed deeply of the pure, cool, oxygen-rich air, looked at the distant mountains, and followed the path upward.

This was a scene I witnessed long ago in a mountain village. I often dreamed of painting it on smooth, sturdy linen canvas. I used to believe this was how an ox's life was meant to be lived — until I visited my friend's cattle farm.

I went to the Northwest and called my friend, arranging to meet after my business was finished. Over dinner, I asked about his work. He told me he was busy setting up a cattle farm. All the cows were premium dairy cattle imported from Australia, averaging nearly two meters in height, with excellent physical condition. They could leap over meter-high fences like wild bison. This piqued my interest. Though the farm was a two-hour drive from where we dined, and I had already booked a flight back to Beijing the next morning, I decided to go and see for myself.

I had never been to Australia, but I had seen reports about these dairy cows on television and in newspapers. These enormous creatures, either strolling leisurely across boundless grasslands, picking the tastiest wild grass; or standing motionless, letting the white clouds drift across the blue sky; or lying on the satiny grass, rolling over; or calves nestled beside their mothers, enjoying a gentle licking — this was the life I imagined for them.

Two hours later, I stood behind a row of fences. The farm was well situated — a river in front with grassy banks, mountains behind with wildflowers blooming on the slopes. But inside the fences, I could not see a single blade of grass. The ground was bare yellow earth. The modest-sized farm held thousands of black-and-white dairy cows. Perhaps for ease of management, rows of stanchions had been set up, like bicycle racks. Each cow was chained to a stall. Their heads could reach through the gaps between the bars to eat from the troughs. The chains were short — even raising their heads was impossible. Cows have no toilets, and there was no exercise yard. They relieved themselves in their stalls. The workers cleaned up the manure daily, but what about the urine? Beneath each cow was a foul, filthy mire of urine mixed with mud. This wet mire, at night, was the only bed these fine dairy cows had to sleep on.

The only time they could move their stiff necks was during the three milking sessions — totaling two hours a day — when workers led them to the milking machines for that brief moment of奉献 to humanity. For the remaining twenty-two hours, these enormous, sentient creatures remained chained in place, producing milk to strengthen the human race. There were no wildflowers, no grass, no open space, not even any sound in the farm — only swarms of flies, like marauding American helicopter squadrons, their violent buzzing cutting through the filthy, exposed barn.

Through my friend's account, I learned about the life of a cow. Newborn calves are separated by sex. Within one to three months, the males are sold to beef farms or local restaurants. Between six months and a year, they are slaughtered for tender veal. Why not raise them longer, to grow bigger and fatter? Because these are not beef cattle — after one year, the feed-to-meat ratio becomes uneconomical. The luckiest among the males are those kept for breeding, but this is rare.

The females are somewhat luckier — at least they are still alive at one year old. Their happiness lasts only two years, from birth until they reach breeding age. Then they are artificially inseminated, with sperm generally imported from the United States or Australia, from some bull they have never met — and they have no choice. Gestation is about ten months. After calving, the mother begins her milk-producing career. The first milk — colostrum — is rich in nutrients and antibodies that boost the calf's immunity. But nowadays, calves rarely get to drink their mother's colostrum. They cannot nestle beside their mothers or feel their mother's tender lick. Newborn calves are immediately separated and taken elsewhere. The mother is deeply distressed — sometimes you can see tears in her eyes. Like any mother, she wants to stay with her baby, to touch its soft fur, to lick its delicate nose, to hear it call for her. But unlike human mothers, she does not belong to her calf. She belongs to humans. She is the farm's asset.

For two months after calving, her milk production is high. After that, it gradually decreases. But humans do not want this. So, one day in the second month, she is artificially inseminated again. Ten months later, she calves again. Again her baby is taken at birth. Again she cries in distress, tears in her eyes, yearning for her baby. Two months later, she is inseminated again. Then calves again. Then separated again. Inseminated again. Calved again. Separated again.

Her life becomes unchanging: insemination — pregnancy — calving — insemination — pregnancy — calving. Until one day, six or seven years into her milking career, when she is no longer young, somewhat tired, and can no longer produce as much milk, she is led straight from her stall, out through the farm gate. Outside, she will see grass she has long missed, smell the sweet wildflowers, hear the murmuring river. But she will never return.

It is hard to know what a cow thinks at such a moment. Will she miss the father she never met, or the mother she saw only once? Will she yearn for the offspring she was never allowed to nurture? Will she remember a gentle breeze that passed by, or the bright moon in the deep blue night? Will she recall the friends who accompanied her through the years? Will she regret never having known love in her lifetime? I do not know the answers. What I do know is this: her stall will be filled by another young cow, and the same hopeless, repetitive cycle will begin again.

I ended up missing my flight the next morning. I imagine the sorrow was too heavy — even a巨大 airliner could not bear to carry it.

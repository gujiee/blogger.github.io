Gu Yuan's ambitious plan to compile a reference work on China's space food never materialized. The increasingly heavy workload of the research lab, along with its特有的 internal politics, consumed most of his energy and confidence. He had no desire to entangle himself in the endless interpersonal strife, but like a withered leaf caught in a great vortex, he could not escape. At least he managed to read through nearly all the classified documents. Beyond their classified content, he discovered certain secrets in the signature logs. He recognized several names: the former director, Shen Yishan — a refined, quiet man who was soon transferred to the ministry — and the current director, nicknamed "The Cannon," who was more like a factory manager than a research institute leader, sharp-tongued and harsh. There was also a researcher from the Second Lab. And one name Gu Yuan did not recognize, though it appeared on every classified document's sign-off list: Ren Kehong.

Before long, the institute's annual conference arrived. As always, everyone lined up outside the auditorium and took their seats by section. The stage bore the institute's emblem, flanked by red flags. Speeches came first, then awards. It was Gu Yuan's first time attending, and he looked around curiously. Each lab entered at the guard's deep, drawn-out command: "Lab Nine, attention. Lab Nine, enter." Gu Yuan's lab was Lab Seven, seated at the back. He could hear every command clearly. After Lab Thirteen entered, he heard the most astonishing command of all: "Lab Fourteen, attention. Lab Fourteen, enter."

When Gu Yuan first started at the institute, a large organizational chart hung on the side of the auxiliary building. At the top was the director, with the political commissar beside him. Below were three deputy directors and a deputy commissar. Further down were the astronaut corps, administrative departments, and finally thirteen research labs. Though Gu Yuan had not been there long, he liked to wander — visiting other labs, chatting with colleagues, complaining about life, making friends with those who had been there longer. But no one had ever mentioned Lab Fourteen.

In astonishment, he turned toward the entrance and noticed many others were also looking. Seven or eight people entered, only one in military uniform; the rest wore civilian clothes. It was a very small lab — the institute had over nine hundred staff, and Gu Yuan's Lab Seven had over thirty, already considered small. Among the civilians was one man frequently seen on videos: the qigong master Zhang Baosheng.

Gu Yuan turned to the senior woman beside him, a veteran of the institute. "Sister, what's Lab Fourteen? I've never heard of it."

She leaned close and whispered, "Lab Fourteen is the institute's last lab. Not publicly disclosed. You'll learn more in time."

After the conference came each lab's tedious year-end meetings, followed by the next year's grand but hollow work plans. Only in the week before the Spring Festival did things finally ease. Because Gu Yuan was new, he did not get the holiday off and was assigned to值班. It was actually a pleasant duty — with everyone else gone, all he had to do was mind the office in case some leader dropped by. The best part was being able to make long-distance calls anytime.

By February 13, everyone except the director had returned. One Friday, Gu Yuan headed to the library after lunch, planning to spend the afternoon watching classified videos. As he climbed the stairs, he found the usually quiet library entrance crowded with people. Unable to get through, he waited on the landing between floors.

An elderly man emerged from the library, surrounded by attendants, and began descending the stairs. He walked slowly. He looked familiar, but Gu Yuan could not place him. As he passed, the old man smiled and nodded at him.

Gu Yuan went into the library and asked the young woman at the front desk. She stared at him as if he were from another planet. "You really don't know?"

"Qian," she said. "Qian Xueqian?"

Gu Yuan's eyes widened. "Qian Xuesen?!"

She smiled knowingly. "He comes nearly every Friday for meetings — sometimes in the small conference room, sometimes in the library lecture hall."

From her, he learned that Qian came to hear Lab Fourteen's research reports. Lab Seven was formally named the Space Food and Spacesuit Lab. Lab Fourteen was called the Subconscious Research Lab — more commonly known as paranormal research.

Besides its director, Lab Fourteen had several extraordinary members, each with特殊 abilities. The lab was not within the main compound but in a highly secluded building outside it — off-limits even to institute staff. Though Lab Fourteen did not appear on the public organizational chart and had fewer than a dozen members, Gu Yuan came to understand a hidden truth: without Lab Fourteen, there would be no 507 Institute.

Later, he learned that his senior colleague's husband also worked in Lab Fourteen — not as a special member, but as Zhang Baosheng's attendant. He was a warm, generous man from Shandong. Once, when the食堂 was serving lunch, the man brought several people with stainless steel basins and loaded up multiple portions of food, placing them in the back of a Jetta and driving off. At the time, Gu Yuan thought nothing of it. Looking back, in 1998, using a Jetta to haul cafeteria food was like using a Rolls-Royce to haul coal — it spoke volumes.

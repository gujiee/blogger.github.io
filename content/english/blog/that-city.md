That city. I cannot say I know it well, though I have passed through it countless times. Every time, it was only a brief encounter — catching sight of its ancient, enduring walls from a train window. Many people told me Xi'an was wonderful, full of places worth visiting. I always meant to spare a day or two on my way to the Northwest to explore it. But until this year, I never fulfilled that wish.

When my wish finally came true, it was sudden, swift, and very formal. Two friends visiting from overseas wanted to see Xi'an, and I volunteered to be their guide. From planning to departure, it took only three days. I picked them up at Beijing's T3 terminal, transferred to T2, and two hours later we landed in Xi'an at noon.

Our accommodation was arranged by a local guide — on the edge of Xincheng Square, facing the Shaanxi Provincial Government building, close to the central Bell and Drum Towers. After a quick meal, we walked to the Drum Tower in the city center, then climbed onto the ancient city wall from the South Gate.

Xi'an's city wall is remarkably intact and imposing. The巨大的 stone bricks, after more than seven hundred years of wind and rain, show only surface erosion. Three or four cars could drive abreast along the top. The wall forms a complete circuit — if so inclined, you could walk the entire 14 kilometers back to where you started. But you can also rent bicycles, available at the South Gate and returnable at other gates — very convenient.

Standing on the wall, you can see the Forest of Steles Park below, surrounded by antique-style buildings. From the blinking neon lights, most of these appeared to be bars. Housing bars in ancient architecture — probably China's most successful example of East-meets-West.

In the archways of the ancient wall stood two burly Northwestern men, dark-faced and pot-bellied, dressed in armor and holding spears. They seemed even more captivating than the wall itself — many foreign children ran up to have their photos taken with them.

The next day's itinerary included the Terracotta Warriors and Huaqing Pool. The Terracotta Warriors were a must-see, at least in my opinion. But Huaqing Pool was closer, so we went there first. By the time we arrived, it was already crowded with visitors. The setting was beautiful, backed by Li Mountain, where I imagine Epang Palace once stood — though now only a few pavilions remain, nothing of the legendary palace described by Du Mu: "Five steps to a tower, ten steps to a pavilion; corridors winding, eaves soaring high; each structure clinging to the terrain, interlocking like a struggle of hearts."

Du Mu wrote nearly 1,500 years ago: "The six states were destroyed by the six states themselves, not by Qin. The Qin clan was destroyed by Qin itself, not by the world. Alas! If the six states had each cherished their people, they could have resisted Qin. If Qin had cherished the people of the six states, it could have passed the throne through three generations, even ten thousand generations — who could have destroyed it? The Qin had no time to mourn themselves, and later generations mourned them; later generations mourned them but did not learn from them, causing yet later generations to mourn them in turn." But over the centuries, which dynasty has been any different? Some say, "History only strikes similar chords, never repeats itself exactly." Others say, "History repeats, but never simply." In my view, these words are too含蓄, uttered by literati and experts under the thumb of power. History simply repeats.

At Huaqing Pool, you cannot miss the bathing pool of Yang Yuhuan, the Tang dynasty beauty. Standing on the viewing platform, I found it hard to imagine that this was the bath of the woman who embodied the ultimate in luxury and imperial favor. By modern standards, it was remarkably modest — indistinguishable from a very ordinary outdoor hot spring. A small pool, its shape pleasant enough, like a flower, but only large enough for one or two people. The surrounding stone was ordinary, no better than common marble. Everything seemed utterly at odds with the legendary golden splendor, music and舞蹈, and exquisite refinement of the Tang golden age.

Visitors hurried past, finding little to see. But not far away, a newly carved white marble statue of Yang Yuhuan had become the center of attention. Almost everyone wanted a photo with her. The statue was slightly larger than life — an ordinary person came up to about her waist. To accentuate her allure after bathing, the statue was draped only in a thin gauze, one smooth leg exposed. Nearly every visitor reached out to touch her bare thigh while taking pictures, lining up in the dry, hot May weather of the Guanzhong plain, tirelessly repeating the ritual. I suspect the sculptor had anticipated this — Yang Yuhuan's face showed neither the comfort of having bathed, nor the dignity of an imperial consort, but rather a trace of melancholy and resignation.

Also at Huaqing Pool, there was supposedly where Empress Dowager Cixi bathed, and where Emperor Tang Minghuang bathed. Also worth seeing, according to our guide, were where "other personnel" bathed — though I suspect he meant eunuchs. And there was the small mountain path where Chiang Kai-shek was said to have taken his famous midnight stroll during the Xi'an Incident. The path was narrow, barely enough for one person. Climbing it on a cold midnight took real courage — especially without shoes. I admired Chiang for that.

At noon we reached the Terracotta Warriors. On the way, we passed a small, even mountain with a flat top — our guide said it was Qin Shihuang's mausoleum. The Terracotta Warriors were just south of it.

The warriors were housed in a few enormous buildings containing the original excavation sites. Pit No. 2 was the most spectacular — the first to be discovered. As we entered, thousands of visitors crowded against the railings, all stretching their necks as if their wallets had fallen below. I found a spot with fewer people, barely enough to stand, and quickly glanced inside. Brief as it was, I was stunned. Before coming, I had seen many photos — most taken in Pit No. 2 — and they looked much like the real thing. But actually, standing there, it was different. Hundreds of fully armed warriors stood in整齐 formation, all roughly the same height, yet each with a different expression and headdress. Each was extraordinarily calm and solemn. Though the battlefield was vast in number, it was utterly silent — eerily so. Fortunately, the crowd was noisy enough to dispel the feeling of being trapped in a terrifying dream.

On the side of Pit No. 2, there were fewer visitors, and I could stop and look properly. Below were human figures and horse figures. The horses drew chariots, but the chariots themselves, being wooden, had long since decayed. From their postures and clothing, the human figures could be divided into light infantry, cavalry, officers, and generals. Despite their number, each figure was exquisitely detailed — down to the gestures and positions of every finger. Even the fingernails had been individually carved. The horse figures were remarkably beautiful. Compared to the crude stone horses of the Han dynasty, the Qin horses were delicate and lifelike. The muscles, the lines of the face — if not for their motionless manes, I would have believed they were real, spirited steeds. These horses and warriors simply stood there — standing for their great emperor for over two thousand years.

On the ride back to Xi'an, the guide told us that Huaqing Pool currently occupies only one-seventh of its original size. The government plans to restore it fully and develop a cluster of villas around the pool and Li Mountain. Though somewhat remote, given the excellent feng shui and the proximity to the legendary beauty's bath, the prices will certainly not be low — at least 10,000 yuan per square meter. Many people are already preparing to order. Excellent idea — developing tourism while boosting the economy — a true killing of two birds with one stone. But if they combined it with the Qin mausoleum and Terracotta Warriors for further development, I think it would be even better. Many schemes come to mind: buy a villa and get a free burial plot near the Qin emperor's tomb; create ceramic figurines based on customers' faces and place them in the pits. Though that would push villa prices well beyond 10,000 — at least 200,000 to 300,000 per square meter, matching the top-tier Beijing properties.

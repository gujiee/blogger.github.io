title: "Giving Up a Seat"

I squeezed onto the Beijing-bound train, gasping for breath. Behind me, people were still surging from the platform into the carriage, but it was already packed — not a single empty space. The departure bell rang. Many outside had not made it on. I heard the conductor cursing: "Hurry up! Push harder, or the train's leaving!" Another surge. Pressure like a wave rolled from the door into the carriage. People inside began shouting: "Stop pushing! Someone's going to die!"

I found a spot — not a seat, but a place where I could put both feet down and stand upright. There was no room for luggage. I had to shove my bag under someone's seat. This was the Guilin-to-Beijing train. My carriage was the last hard-seat car, next to the dining car. In my experience, the dining car was always a good option — pay a little and sit comfortably all the way to Beijing. But this train exceeded my expectations. The door between the hard-seat car and the dining car was tightly locked. Passengers piled up against the locked door as if it did not exist. The steel wall and the crowd had cut off any passage.

With no other option, maintaining the status quo was good enough. I hoped that at Xinxiang or Anyang, enough people would get off to make it more comfortable...

On the train, I met a boy, eight or nine years old, squeezed in the crowd beside me. "Uncle, are you getting off soon?" he asked.

"No, I'm going all the way to Beijing. Are you?"

"I'm getting off at the next stop. My dad is picking me up."

"Where does your dad live?"

"Zhengzhou."

"Are you traveling alone?"

"Yes."

I could not help but notice his small, thin body pressed against the crowd. "Here," I said, "sit on my bag."

He hesitated, then squatted down on my bag, his small hands clutching his knees. After a while, the train stopped at a station. People got on and off, but the carriage remained packed. The boy looked up and said, "Uncle, are you tired? You can sit for a while."

"No, I'm fine. You sit."

The boy looked at me with his clear, dark eyes, wanting to say something, but the train started moving again. He looked down and was silent.

After Zhengzhou, I finally found a seat. I sat by the window, watching the receding station buildings and pedestrians. The boy's small figure had long since disappeared into the crowd. I did not even know his name.

I leaned back, feeling the fatigue of standing for so many hours. Suddenly, I thought: giving up a seat — is it really so difficult?

At the next station, when an elderly man boarded with difficulty, I stood up without hesitation.
